Microsoft Warns of Payroll Pirate Attacks in Canada as Token Theft Turns Microsoft 365 Into a Direct Deposit Target

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-04-10

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Microsoft says Storm-2755 is hijacking employee sessions to reroute payroll deposits in Canada.
  • The attacker uses adversary-in-the-middle phishing to steal tokens and bypass traditional MFA protections.
  • This is another sign that identity defense now has to focus on session integrity, not just passwords.

What Happened

Microsoft says a financially motivated threat actor tracked as Storm-2755 is targeting Canadian employees in payroll pirate attacks that redirect salary payments after account compromise. The method is ugly because it is practical. Attackers lure victims to fake Microsoft 365 sign-in pages, capture authentication tokens and session cookies, then use the authenticated access to tamper with payroll workflows.

After gaining access, the actor reportedly creates inbox rules to hide messages containing terms such as direct deposit or bank, then impersonates employees to contact HR staff or logs into payroll systems directly. The objective is not espionage. It is fast, monetizable fraud using trust already built into workplace identity systems.

Background and Context

This fits a broader shift in phishing operations. Attackers increasingly do not care only about usernames and passwords. They want sessions, tokens, consent grants, and any artifact that lets them step past the login screen as if they are the legitimate user. Adversary-in-the-middle phishing has become more dangerous precisely because many organizations still think in older authentication terms.

Microsoft 365 is a particularly attractive target because it sits at the center of email, approvals, calendars, HR communication, and employee identity. Once attackers have access, they can read context, conceal activity, and make requests that look routine. In payroll fraud, that context is enough to turn a stolen session into stolen wages.

Why This Matters

This matters because it shows how identity abuse is now a workflow abuse problem. A compromised mailbox is not only a mailbox problem. It can become a finance problem, an HR problem, and a trust problem. Organizations that still rely on basic MFA plus user training are exposed if they do not have stronger session protections and faster incident response.

The practical reality is that businesses need secure foundations across endpoints and productivity systems too. Managed devices, controlled browser sessions, and a reliable stack built on a genuine Windows 11 key and an affordable Microsoft Office licence do not solve token theft alone, but they help create an environment where stronger policy controls are actually enforceable.

Industry Impact and Competitive Landscape

Microsoft is using this warning to push defenders toward phishing-resistant MFA and tighter identity controls, and it is right to do so. Identity vendors across the market are making the same argument because the threat data supports it. The era when app-based MFA was treated as a final answer is ending.

The lesson extends beyond Microsoft too. Any productivity suite or SSO environment that concentrates communication and approvals becomes a fraud platform when authentication is compromised. Attackers will keep targeting wherever trust is densest.

Expert Perspective

The key shift is from credential theft to session theft. Defenders who still measure success mainly by password resets are solving yesterday’s problem. The attacker wants continuity, not just a login.

What This Means for Businesses

Businesses should review mailbox rule monitoring, token revocation procedures, HR verification steps for payroll changes, and their use of phishing-resistant authentication. This is not a niche Canadian story. It is a template for how business email compromise is evolving.

Key Takeaways

  • Storm-2755 is using Microsoft 365 session theft to reroute payroll deposits in Canada.
  • Adversary-in-the-middle phishing defeats legacy MFA assumptions by capturing authenticated sessions.
  • Identity defense now has to focus on sessions, tokens, and workflow abuse.
  • Payroll and HR processes need stronger verification controls.

Looking Ahead

Expect more financially motivated groups to copy this model because it is effective, scalable, and hard for victims to notice quickly. Session integrity is becoming one of the most important front lines in enterprise security.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do these payroll attacks work?

The attacker steals authenticated Microsoft 365 sessions, hides payroll correspondence, and then changes direct deposit details.

Does MFA stop this?

Not reliably if the attacker captures session cookies or OAuth tokens in real time.

What is the best defense?

Use phishing-resistant MFA, tighten conditional access, and respond quickly by revoking sessions and tokens after suspected compromise.

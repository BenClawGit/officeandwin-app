ZeroEyes Expands AI Threat Detection Beyond Guns to Knives and Suspect Tracking

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-04-03

⚡ Quick Summary

  • ZeroEyes expands AI detection from guns to knives, suspect tracking, and behavioral analysis
  • Platform now covers 30+ weapon types across 4,000+ US locations
  • Human-in-the-loop review maintained for all AI-generated alerts
  • Raises questions about balance between public safety and surveillance

What Happened

ZeroEyes Inc., the computer vision startup known for its AI-based gun detection technology deployed across schools, hospitals, and public venues, has announced a major platform expansion that transforms it from a single-purpose weapon detection tool into a comprehensive physical security intelligence system. The update adds knife detection, suspect tracking across multiple camera feeds, and behavioral analysis capabilities designed to identify potential threats before weapons are even drawn.

The expanded platform processes feeds from existing security camera infrastructure, applying real-time AI analysis to identify weapons, track individuals flagged as potential threats across a building or campus, and alert security personnel with actionable intelligence including location, direction of movement, and threat assessment. The system now supports over 30 weapon types beyond firearms, including bladed weapons, improvised weapons, and concealed weapon indicators identified through behavioral analysis.

ZeroEyes emphasizes that its system is designed to augment human security teams, not replace them. Every AI-generated alert is reviewed by the company's operations center staffed by military and law enforcement veterans before notifications are sent to local security or first responders. This human-in-the-loop approach has been central to ZeroEyes' value proposition, reducing false positive rates to levels that security teams can trust.

Background and Context

ZeroEyes was founded in 2018 by a team of military veterans and computer vision engineers with the initial mission of reducing response times to active shooter situations. The company's original product analyzed security camera feeds to identify visible firearms and alert authorities within seconds — dramatically faster than the minutes or hours it can take for human observers to notice and report a weapon.

The company has secured deployments across more than 4,000 locations in the United States, including K-12 schools, universities, hospitals, corporate campuses, and government buildings. Its growth has been fueled by the persistent threat of mass violence in the U.S. and growing frustration with traditional security approaches that rely on reactive response rather than proactive detection.

The expansion into knife detection and suspect tracking responds to market demand from customers who found that gun detection alone was insufficient. Knife attacks, while less frequently covered by national media, are statistically more common than firearm attacks in many settings. And security teams wanted the ability to track a flagged individual as they moved through a facility, rather than receiving a single point-in-time alert.

The broader AI security market has grown rapidly, with competitors including Evolv Technology (which focuses on weapons screening at entry points), Athena Security, and Scylla AI. ZeroEyes differentiates through its software-only approach that works with existing cameras, eliminating the need for specialized hardware installation.

Why This Matters

The expansion of AI surveillance capabilities into comprehensive threat intelligence raises important questions about the balance between public safety and civil liberties. ZeroEyes' technology can now not only identify weapons but track individuals across locations and analyze behavior patterns — capabilities that, if misused, could enable surveillance overreach.

ZeroEyes has attempted to address these concerns through its human-in-the-loop model and by explicitly declining to offer facial recognition capabilities. The company's stated position is that its system identifies threats (weapons and threatening behavior), not identities. However, the suspect tracking feature — which follows an individual across camera feeds based on appearance characteristics — approaches identity tracking in practical terms, even if it technically doesn't use facial recognition databases.

For organizations evaluating physical security solutions, this expansion makes ZeroEyes a more compelling but also more complex proposition. The broader the AI's detection capabilities, the more potential there is for both genuine security improvement and unintended consequences. Businesses investing in enterprise productivity software and physical security infrastructure need to evaluate AI security tools with the same rigor they apply to cybersecurity solutions.

Industry Impact

ZeroEyes' platform expansion signals that the AI physical security market is evolving from point solutions to integrated platforms. Rather than deploying separate systems for gun detection, knife detection, behavioral analysis, and suspect tracking, organizations can increasingly rely on unified AI platforms that provide comprehensive threat intelligence from existing camera infrastructure.

This consolidation trend mirrors what happened in cybersecurity, where dozens of point solutions were eventually consolidated into integrated security platforms by companies like CrowdStrike, Palo Alto Networks, and Microsoft. The physical security market appears to be entering a similar consolidation phase, where the companies that offer the most comprehensive AI capabilities will absorb market share from single-purpose competitors.

For the education sector, which represents a significant portion of ZeroEyes' customer base, the expanded capabilities address a recurring criticism: that gun detection alone arrives too late in the threat timeline. Behavioral analysis and tracking capabilities could enable intervention before a weapon is displayed, potentially preventing incidents rather than merely accelerating response to them.

The insurance industry is also watching these developments closely. Commercial property and liability insurers have begun offering premium discounts for facilities that deploy AI-based security systems, recognizing that these tools measurably reduce incident severity and response times. As AI security capabilities expand, expect insurance incentives to grow correspondingly.

Expert Perspective

Security technology analysts note that ZeroEyes' expansion represents a natural evolution but also a threshold moment. A system that detects visible firearms is relatively uncontroversial — few argue against identifying an active shooter faster. But a system that tracks individuals, analyzes behavior, and detects concealed weapons through pattern analysis enters more contentious territory where the line between security and surveillance becomes blurred.

The human-in-the-loop model provides an important safeguard, but its effectiveness depends on the quality and independence of the human reviewers. As alert volumes increase with broader detection capabilities, there's a risk that human reviewers become rubber stamps for AI decisions rather than genuine oversight mechanisms. ZeroEyes will need to demonstrate that its operations center maintains genuinely independent review as the platform scales.

What This Means for Businesses

Organizations responsible for physical security — whether schools, hospitals, corporate offices, or retail locations — should evaluate how AI threat detection could complement their existing security measures. The technology has matured significantly from its early experimental phase and now offers proven capabilities backed by extensive real-world deployment data.

Looking Ahead

ZeroEyes is expected to continue expanding its detection capabilities while navigating the delicate balance between security effectiveness and privacy concerns. The company has indicated interest in international markets, where regulatory requirements around AI surveillance vary significantly. As AI security technology matures, expect growing regulatory attention — particularly in the EU, where the AI Act imposes specific requirements on AI systems used for biometric identification and surveillance in public spaces.

Nearly Half of US AI Data Centres Face Delays as Tariffs on Chinese Equipment Choke Power Infrastructure

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-04-03

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Nearly half of US data centres planned for 2026 face delays due to inability to import Chinese power infrastructure components
  • Critical electrical equipment delivery times have stretched from 24-30 months to up to five years
  • Only one-third of the largest planned AI data centres are currently under construction
  • The situation creates a strategic paradox between trade policy goals and AI infrastructure needs

What Happened

The ambitious US push to rapidly build out AI data centre capacity is running headlong into an infrastructure wall of its own making. According to a Bloomberg investigation, almost half of the data centres planned for completion in 2026 are expected to be delayed or cancelled because developers cannot import enough critical power infrastructure components — transformers, switchgear, and batteries — that have been primarily manufactured in China for US markets for decades.

The bottleneck is severe. Components that once took 24 to 30 months for delivery before 2020 now face wait times of up to five years. Market intelligence firm Sightline Climate estimates that only a third of the largest AI data centres scheduled to come online this year are currently under construction. Many developers, caught between project timelines and import restrictions, are reportedly willing to pay steep tariffs and accept alleged national security concerns just to secure equipment from Chinese suppliers on faster timelines.

The situation represents a painful irony: aggressive tariffs on Chinese imports, designed in part to boost US manufacturing and protect national interests, are directly undermining another stated priority — ensuring America leads the global AI race through rapid data centre deployment. US manufacturing capacity for these critical electrical components simply cannot keep up with the unprecedented demand driven by the AI boom.

Background and Context

The AI data centre buildout has been positioned as a national priority through a series of executive orders emphasising rapid construction to maintain US competitiveness against China in artificial intelligence. The logic is straightforward: advanced AI models require enormous computing power, computing power requires data centres, and whoever builds the most data centre capacity fastest gains a structural advantage in AI development.

But data centres don't just need servers and cooling systems — they need massive amounts of electrical power, and the infrastructure to deliver that power is where the supply chain breaks down. High-voltage transformers, switchgear panels, and industrial battery systems are specialised components that require significant manufacturing expertise and capacity. China has dominated this manufacturing space for decades, building the factories, training the workforce, and scaling production to meet global demand.

A March executive order directed tech companies to "build, bring, or buy" the power for their data centres, but conspicuously avoided addressing the fundamental problem: it doesn't matter where the power comes from if the electrical infrastructure to distribute and manage it can't be sourced. Meanwhile, multiple communities and municipalities across the US have begun implementing data centre moratoriums, citing concerns about power grid strain, water usage, and local environmental impact.

Why This Matters

The data centre bottleneck has cascading implications for the entire technology ecosystem. Every delayed data centre represents delayed AI computing capacity, which in turn slows AI model development, limits the rollout of AI-powered services, and constrains the ability of cloud providers to meet surging enterprise demand for AI infrastructure.

For the businesses and workers who depend on reliable cloud computing and AI services — including those running affordable Microsoft Office licence deployments with cloud-connected features — this means potential capacity constraints and price increases as demand outstrips available compute. Major cloud providers like Microsoft Azure, Amazon Web Services, and Google Cloud have all announced massive data centre expansion plans, and any delays ripple through to the millions of businesses that rely on their services.

The geopolitical dimension is equally significant. The AI race between the US and China is increasingly framed as a strategic contest with economic and security implications on par with the space race or nuclear arms competition. The paradox of US trade policy simultaneously trying to outcompete China in AI while restricting imports of the very components needed to build AI infrastructure creates a strategic tension that will need resolution. China is estimated to be roughly five years behind the US in AI development — coincidentally similar to the current wait time for the power infrastructure components that the US needs.

Industry Impact

The immediate impact falls on data centre developers and the hyperscale cloud providers that are their primary customers. Companies that locked in equipment orders early or secured domestic supply chains are in a stronger competitive position than those still waiting on Chinese imports. This is creating a two-tier market where some data centre projects proceed on schedule while others face indefinite delays.

The situation is driving renewed interest in domestic manufacturing of electrical infrastructure components. Several initiatives are underway to build US-based transformer and switchgear manufacturing capacity, but these facilities take years to plan, build, and bring to full production. Even with accelerated timelines and government incentives, domestic manufacturing won't close the supply gap in 2026 or 2027.

Alternative energy solutions are also gaining attention. Some data centre developers are exploring on-site power generation through natural gas turbines, small modular nuclear reactors, or large-scale battery installations that reduce dependence on grid-connected transformer infrastructure. Microsoft, for example, has invested in nuclear power partnerships, while others are looking at direct connections to renewable energy sources that bypass some traditional grid infrastructure.

The financial implications are substantial. Data centre development projects represent billions of dollars in investment, and delays translate directly into delayed returns, increased carrying costs, and potential penalty clauses in contracts with anchor tenants. Some investors are beginning to reassess the risk profile of data centre investments in light of the infrastructure bottleneck.

Expert Perspective

Infrastructure analysts see the current situation as a predictable consequence of underinvestment in domestic manufacturing combined with sudden, explosive demand growth. The US electrical equipment manufacturing base has been declining for years as production shifted to lower-cost markets, primarily China. The AI boom created demand that would have strained supply chains even without trade restrictions — the tariffs simply made an existing problem dramatically worse.

Some analysts argue that the pain is temporary and will ultimately strengthen the US position by forcing the development of domestic supply chains. Others counter that the AI race is happening now, and a five-year timeline to build domestic manufacturing capacity means conceding significant ground to competitors who don't face the same constraints. The optimal path likely involves a pragmatic combination of targeted tariff exemptions for critical infrastructure components, accelerated domestic manufacturing investment, and creative engineering solutions that reduce dependence on constrained equipment.

What This Means for Businesses

Companies planning to expand their cloud computing and AI capabilities should factor infrastructure constraints into their timelines. Cloud capacity that was expected to be available in late 2026 or 2027 may materialise later than planned, potentially affecting project schedules and budgets. Organisations should engage early with their cloud providers to understand capacity commitments and explore whether enterprise productivity software workloads can be optimised to reduce compute requirements.

For businesses running on-premises infrastructure with genuine Windows 11 key deployments, the data centre bottleneck is a reminder that cloud migration timelines may need adjustment. Having robust local computing capabilities provides a buffer against potential cloud capacity constraints, making a balanced hybrid approach increasingly attractive.

Key Takeaways

  • Nearly 50% of US data centres planned for 2026 face delays or cancellation due to power infrastructure shortages
  • Critical electrical components from China now require up to five years for delivery, compared to 24-30 months pre-2020
  • Only one-third of the largest AI data centres due this year are currently under construction
  • US domestic manufacturing capacity for transformers and switchgear cannot meet demand
  • Some developers are paying tariffs and accepting security concerns to expedite Chinese equipment
  • Community moratoriums on data centre construction are adding further complications

Looking Ahead

The US data centre bottleneck is unlikely to resolve quickly. Building domestic manufacturing capacity for critical electrical infrastructure takes years, and the AI-driven demand for data centre construction shows no signs of slowing. Expect continued tension between trade policy objectives and infrastructure development needs, with potential targeted exemptions for data centre equipment as the political costs of delayed AI capacity become more apparent. The companies and countries that navigate this supply chain challenge most effectively will shape the next chapter of the AI revolution.

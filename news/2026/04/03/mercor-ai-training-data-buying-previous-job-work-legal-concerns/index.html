AI Training Data Giant Mercor Sparks Legal Concerns by Offering to Buy Workers' Previous Job Materials

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-04-03

⚡ Quick Summary

  • AI data company Mercor reportedly offers to buy workers' proprietary materials from previous jobs for AI training
  • The practice raises serious legal concerns around intellectual property theft and employment contract violations
  • Visual effects artists and specialists have been targeted for domain-specific production materials
  • The story highlights the AI industry's increasingly aggressive scramble for high-quality training data

What Happened

AI training data company Mercor is reportedly approaching professionals across multiple industries — including visual effects, engineering, and other specialised fields — with offers to purchase work materials from their previous jobs. According to a Wall Street Journal investigation, Mercor has been asking workers for highly specialised content such as "4D physics scenes with camera data, depth and motion/point tracking" — the kind of domain-specific material that is extremely difficult to obtain through conventional means and would be invaluable for training AI models with real-world expertise.

The practice raises immediate legal red flags. Much of the work that Mercor is reportedly seeking likely belongs to the employers for whom it was originally produced. Workers in specialised industries are typically bound by intellectual property agreements, non-disclosure clauses, and employment contracts that explicitly prevent them from sharing proprietary work materials with third parties. The legal ownership of work product created during employment is well-established in most jurisdictions.

While Mercor stated that it "does not buy intellectual property," the Journal reported that messages sent to potential sellers included the phrase "looking to purchase" — language that seems difficult to reconcile with the company's public position. The distinction between purchasing someone's work product and purchasing intellectual property is likely to be tested if any of these transactions lead to legal challenges from original employers.

Background and Context

Mercor has built a substantial business by paying for domain expertise — recruiting professionals with specific industry knowledge to contribute specialised data that generic training datasets can't provide. The company has been particularly active in recruiting people who have lost their jobs, offering paid opportunities to contribute their expertise to AI training. This business model has made Mercor one of the most important vendors in the AI supply chain, with clients including Meta, OpenAI, and Anthropic.

The hunger for high-quality training data is driven by a fundamental reality of AI development: the quality of training data is as important as model architecture in determining AI performance. General-purpose web-scraped data has diminishing returns as AI models become more capable, and AI labs are increasingly seeking specialised, domain-specific data that can give their models expertise in areas like engineering, medicine, law, and creative production. This demand has created a gold rush mentality among data sourcing companies, with Mercor at the leading edge.

The approach of sourcing data from former employees is a natural extension of Mercor's existing strategy, but it crosses into legally treacherous territory. Employment law, intellectual property law, and trade secret protections all converge to create significant risk for both Mercor and the workers who sell materials from previous jobs. The fact that Mercor appears to be pursuing this strategy despite these risks suggests the competitive pressure to secure unique training data is intense enough to justify the legal exposure.

Why This Matters

This story illuminates the increasingly desperate scramble for training data that's driving some AI companies toward legally and ethically questionable practices. As the most accessible sources of training data — public web content, open datasets, licensed archives — become exhausted or contested, the pressure to find novel data sources intensifies. Mercor's approach represents a concerning frontier where the AI industry's data needs collide with workers' contractual obligations and employers' intellectual property rights.

For workers considering Mercor's offers, the risks are substantial. Selling proprietary work materials from a previous employer could constitute breach of contract, theft of trade secrets, or violation of non-disclosure agreements — any of which could result in lawsuits, financial penalties, or criminal charges depending on the jurisdiction and the nature of the materials involved. The short-term payment from Mercor is unlikely to compensate for the potential legal liability. This situation underscores the importance of understanding employment agreements — much like understanding affordable Microsoft Office licence terms, knowing what you've agreed to in contracts prevents costly surprises.

Industry Impact

Mercor's data sourcing practices could trigger a wave of legal action from companies whose former employees have sold proprietary materials. Studios in the visual effects industry, engineering firms, and other specialised sectors may need to audit their intellectual property protections and take legal action if they discover that proprietary work has been sold for AI training purposes.

The situation also puts pressure on AI labs that use Mercor's services. If training data obtained through Mercor includes material that was illegally sourced from previous employers, the AI labs that use that data could face derivative legal liability. This echoes the ongoing legal battles over AI training data sourced from copyrighted web content, but with the added complication of employment law and trade secret violations.

For the broader AI industry, the Mercor story is a cautionary tale about the sustainability of current data sourcing practices. The industry needs to develop data sourcing standards that are legally defensible, ethically sound, and commercially viable — a combination that's proving increasingly difficult to achieve as the easy sources of training data run dry.

The visual effects industry, which the Journal specifically mentioned as a target for Mercor's outreach, has already been vocal about the threat AI poses to creative workers' livelihoods. Learning that a company is actively trying to purchase their proprietary work to train AI systems that could replace them adds insult to injury and is likely to intensify industry opposition to AI data sourcing practices.

Expert Perspective

Intellectual property attorneys view Mercor's practice as legally risky for all parties involved. Even if Mercor structures its purchases to avoid directly acquiring "intellectual property" in a narrow legal sense, the functional reality of buying someone's previous work product for AI training purposes will likely be viewed by courts as substantively equivalent to purchasing IP. The legal distinction Mercor is attempting to draw may not survive judicial scrutiny.

The precedent set by how this situation resolves will have lasting implications. If Mercor faces significant legal consequences, it could deter other data companies from pursuing similar strategies. If the practice goes unchallenged, it could normalise a data sourcing model that undermines employment agreements and intellectual property protections across every industry.

What This Means for Businesses

Companies with valuable intellectual property should review their employment agreements, NDA provisions, and IP assignment clauses to ensure they adequately protect against former employees selling proprietary materials to AI training companies. Organisations using enterprise productivity software to create valuable work product should ensure that exit procedures include clear communication about continuing obligations and that IP protections extend to the specialised data and methodologies that AI companies increasingly value.

Businesses should also be aware that the AI tools they use may have been trained on data of questionable provenance. While this doesn't create direct liability for end users, it contributes to an ecosystem where IP protections are weakened. Supporting industry initiatives for transparent, ethical AI data sourcing benefits everyone who creates valuable intellectual property — which, in today's economy, includes virtually every knowledge worker running genuine Windows 11 key workstations.

  • Visual effects artists and other specialists have been targeted for domain-specific materials

Looking Ahead

The Mercor story is likely to accelerate calls for comprehensive AI data sourcing regulations that establish clear rules about what training data can be legally acquired and how. Expect to see increased litigation from companies discovering their proprietary materials in AI training datasets, stronger employment agreement provisions addressing AI-era data risks, and potentially federal legislation addressing the AI training data supply chain. The era of "move fast and acquire data" is giving way to a more scrutinised landscape where provenance and legality matter as much as quality and relevance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it legal to sell work from a previous job to AI companies?

In most cases, no. Work produced during employment typically belongs to the employer under IP assignment clauses in employment contracts. Workers are usually bound by non-disclosure agreements and trade secret protections that prohibit sharing proprietary materials with third parties. Selling such materials could result in lawsuits, financial penalties, or criminal charges.

What is Mercor?

Mercor is a leading AI training data company that hires networks of contractors to generate specialised datasets for major AI labs including Meta, OpenAI, and Anthropic. The company focuses on domain expertise, paying professionals to contribute industry-specific knowledge for AI model training. It is reportedly now approaching professionals to purchase materials from their previous employment.

How does this affect AI companies using Mercor's data?

AI labs that use training data obtained through potentially illegal means could face derivative legal liability. If Mercor's data includes materials sourced in violation of employment contracts or IP agreements, the AI companies that train models on this data may face legal challenges from the original rights holders.

Iran Internet Blackout Crosses 1,000 Hours as Military-Grade Jamming Targets Starlink

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-04-13

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Iran's nationwide internet blackout has passed 1,000 hours, making it the second-longest country-scale shutdown ever recorded
  • The regime is using military-grade signal jamming against Starlink terminals, and possessing satellite internet equipment is now punishable by death
  • The blackout has severed access to essential services including banking, healthcare, and education for Iran's 88 million citizens
  • The unprecedented shutdown highlights the vulnerability of internet infrastructure to government control and the limits of satellite internet as a circumvention tool

Iran's nationwide internet blackout has crossed the 1,000-hour mark, making it the second-longest country-scale internet shutdown ever measured, surpassed only by Myanmar's 18-month military junta blackout. The shutdown, which began amid domestic unrest and international tensions, has effectively cut Iran's 88 million citizens off from the global internet, with devastating consequences for daily life, commerce, and communication.

The regime's determination to maintain the blackout is evident in its aggressive countermeasures against circumvention technologies. Military-grade signal jamming is being deployed against Starlink satellite internet terminals, according to multiple technical assessments from outside Iran. The jamming operates on frequencies used by Starlink's Ku-band downlink, effectively drowning out the satellite signal within Iranian territory.

The stakes for Iranians attempting to use Starlink have been raised to an extreme level. The Iranian government has declared possession of unauthorized satellite internet terminals a capital offense. Multiple reports indicate that individuals caught with Starlink hardware face the death penalty, a punishment that the regime has carried out in previous cases involving satellite television equipment. The threat has effectively suppressed grassroots Starlink deployment despite the technology's potential to bypass terrestrial internet controls.

The impact on ordinary Iranians has been catastrophic. Banking systems that rely on international connectivity have been severely disrupted, preventing cross-border transactions and hampering domestic electronic payments. Healthcare providers cannot access international medical databases or communicate with specialists abroad. University students and researchers are cut off from global academic resources. Businesses that depend on international trade and communication have been forced to halt operations.

From a technology and cybersecurity perspective, the Iranian blackout demonstrates both the vulnerability of centralized internet infrastructure to government control and the current limitations of satellite internet as a circumvention tool. Starlink's effectiveness depends on the absence of sophisticated jamming, and when a state deploys military-grade electronic warfare against satellite signals, even the most advanced consumer satellite internet service cannot guarantee access.

The situation has reignited debate about internet resilience architecture. Technologies like mesh networking, delayed-delivery protocols, and peer-to-peer communication systems could provide basic connectivity even during shutdowns, but these require pre-deployment and community organization that is difficult to achieve under authoritarian conditions. For the global technology community, Iran's blackout is a stark reminder that internet access should never be taken for granted.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long has Iran's internet been shut down?

Over 1,000 hours and counting, making it the second-longest country-scale internet shutdown ever recorded.

Can Starlink work in Iran?

Starlink can theoretically provide internet access in Iran, but the regime is using military-grade signal jamming targeting Starlink frequencies, rendering terminals ineffective in most areas.

What are the penalties for using Starlink in Iran?

Possessing unauthorized satellite internet terminals has been declared a capital offense punishable by death, effectively suppressing grassroots Starlink deployment.

How are Iranians communicating during the blackout?

Limited communication occurs through SMS, satellite television broadcasts from outside Iran, physical media, and occasional brief windows when partial connectivity is restored.

Has this happened before in other countries?

Myanmar holds the record for the longest shutdown at 18 months. India, Egypt, and Ethiopia have also implemented significant internet blackouts, though none as long as Iran's current outage.

What can the international tech community do?

Support development of censorship-resistant technologies, fund VPN and proxy services, apply diplomatic pressure for internet freedom, and invest in alternative communication infrastructure like mesh networks.

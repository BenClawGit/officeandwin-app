Social Media Scams Cost Americans $2.1 Billion in 2025, FTC Reports

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-04-28

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Social media scams cost Americans $2.1 billion in 2025, according to the Federal Trade Commission's latest report.
  • Facebook was the most commonly reported platform for scam activity, followed by Instagram and WhatsApp.
  • Investment scams and romance fraud were the highest-loss categories on social platforms.
  • The FTC is pushing platforms to implement stronger verification and fraud detection systems.

The Federal Trade Commission has released its annual report on fraud in the United States, and the numbers for social media-driven scams are staggering: Americans lost $2.1 billion to scams originating on or facilitated by social media platforms in 2025. Facebook was the most reported platform, accounting for the largest share of complaints and financial losses.

The Scale of the Problem

$2.1 billion in a single year from social media scams alone is a figure that demands attention. For context, this represents a continued year-over-year increase — social media scam losses have roughly tripled since 2021, driven by both the growing user base on these platforms and the increasing sophistication of scam operations. The FTC notes that the actual figure is almost certainly higher, since many victims never report their losses due to embarrassment or lack of awareness about reporting mechanisms.

The median individual loss was $700, but the distribution is heavily skewed: investment scams on social media produced a median loss of $3,800, with some individual losses exceeding $100,000. Romance scams — where perpetrators build fabricated romantic relationships over weeks or months before requesting money — produced a median loss of $2,600. These are not victimless or trivial crimes; they represent life-altering financial losses for many victims.

Why Social Media Is the Preferred Attack Surface

Social media platforms are designed to build trust quickly and encourage rapid interaction. Features like mutual connections, group memberships, and verified badges create a false sense of legitimacy that scammers exploit. The platforms' recommendation algorithms, designed to maximise engagement, frequently surface scam content to users who are most likely to engage with it — older adults for investment scams, emotionally vulnerable users for romance fraud, deal-seekers for shopping scams.

Facebook's dominance in the FTC data reflects both its enormous user base and the breadth of its feature set. Facebook Marketplace, Groups, Pages, and Messenger each provide distinct attack surfaces that scammers exploit. A single scammer can create a fake business Page, post fraudulent listings on Marketplace, join relevant Groups to find targets, and move the conversation to Messenger — all within Facebook's ecosystem, and all using the platform's own tools for legitimate business and social interaction.

The Platform Response

Meta, which owns Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp, has invested significantly in automated scam detection — using AI to identify and remove suspicious accounts, fake listings, and fraudulent advertisements. The company reports removing millions of scam accounts quarterly. But the FTC data indicates that these efforts are not keeping pace with the scale of the problem. Scammers adapt faster than detection systems can respond, and the economic incentive to keep trying — $2.1 billion is an attractive revenue pool — ensures a continuous supply of threat actors.

The FTC has been increasingly aggressive in pushing for stronger platform accountability. In its latest report, the commission explicitly calls on social media companies to implement more rigorous identity verification for business accounts and advertisers, improve the speed of scam content removal, provide clearer risk warnings to users engaging with unfamiliar accounts, and increase transparency about the scale and types of scam activity on their platforms.

For Enterprise and IT Teams

While the FTC report focuses on consumer losses, the implications for enterprise security are real. Employees who are susceptible to social media scams in their personal lives are also susceptible to professionally targeted social engineering. The same trust-building and urgency tactics that work in romance and investment scams work in business email compromise and credential phishing. Security awareness training programmes should explicitly address social media scam patterns — not just email phishing — as the psychological manipulation techniques are identical across channels.

Frequently Asked Questions

What types of scams are most common on social media?

The FTC reports investment scams as the highest-loss category, followed by romance scams, shopping fraud, and business impersonation. Social platforms' design — which encourages trust and rapid interaction — makes users more susceptible.

Why is Facebook the most reported platform?

Facebook's enormous user base, marketplace features, groups, and messaging tools provide scammers with the largest possible target audience and multiple attack surfaces.

How can individuals protect themselves?

Verify identities independently before engaging financially, never send money to people you have only met online, use platform-reported fraud tools, and enable two-factor authentication on all social accounts.

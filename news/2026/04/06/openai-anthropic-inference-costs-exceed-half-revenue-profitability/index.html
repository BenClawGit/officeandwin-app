OpenAI and Anthropic Reveal Inference Costs Exceed Half of Revenue in Investor Documents

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-04-06

⚡ Quick Summary

  • OpenAI and Anthropic inference costs exceed half of total revenue per confidential investor documents
  • Both companies present profitability projections with and without training costs to investors
  • The AI industry's 'grow now, profit later' model faces sustainability questions
  • Businesses should plan for potential AI service price increases and evaluate open-source alternatives

What Happened

Confidential investor documents from OpenAI and Anthropic have revealed a striking financial reality at the heart of the AI industry: inference costs — the expense of actually running AI models to process user queries — now exceed half of total revenue at both companies. The Wall Street Journal obtained documents showing that both companies have presented profitability projections to investors that separate training costs from inference costs, painting different pictures of financial health depending on which costs are included.

The documents show that while both companies are growing revenue rapidly, the marginal cost of serving each user query remains stubbornly high. Every conversation with ChatGPT, every API call to Claude, every enterprise Copilot interaction consumes significant compute resources. As usage scales, so do inference costs — creating a situation where revenue growth does not automatically translate into improved profitability.

This disclosure challenges the narrative that AI companies will achieve profitability simply by scaling usage. The traditional software model — where marginal costs approach zero as user bases grow — does not apply to AI inference, where each interaction requires real-time computation on expensive GPU hardware. Both companies have projected pathways to profitability, but the timelines and assumptions vary significantly depending on whether training costs are included in the calculation.

Background and Context

The AI industry has attracted hundreds of billions of dollars in investment based on the expectation that artificial intelligence will transform computing and generate enormous returns. OpenAI has raised over $30 billion in funding at valuations exceeding $300 billion, while Anthropic has secured over $15 billion from investors including Google, Amazon, and Salesforce. These valuations assume eventual profitability at massive scale.

The economics of AI are fundamentally different from traditional software. Building a software application involves upfront development costs, but once built, each additional user costs almost nothing to serve. AI models, by contrast, require continuous investment in both training (building and improving models) and inference (running models to serve users). Training costs are periodic but enormous — GPT-5 scale models are estimated to cost over $1 billion to train. Inference costs are ongoing and scale linearly with usage.

The GPU supply chain adds another layer of complexity. Nvidia's data centre GPUs — essential for both training and inference — remain in high demand, with lead times of months and prices in the tens of thousands of dollars per unit. While inference can run on less powerful hardware than training, serving millions of concurrent users still requires massive data centre infrastructure that represents both capital expenditure and ongoing operational costs.

Why This Matters

The inference cost problem has profound implications for the sustainability of the current AI business model. If serving AI queries remains expensive, the industry faces a difficult choice: either raise prices to cover costs (potentially limiting adoption), accept lower margins than traditional software companies (disappointing investors), or find technological breakthroughs that dramatically reduce inference costs (uncertain timeline).

For enterprise customers who have integrated AI tools into their workflows, the economics matter because they affect pricing stability and service continuity. Businesses that have built processes around AI-powered tools — from coding assistants to customer service agents to productivity features in suites like affordable Microsoft Office licence deployments — need confidence that these services will remain available and affordable over time. If AI providers cannot achieve sustainable economics, service disruptions or significant price increases become real risks.

The investor documents also reveal an important accounting distinction. By presenting profitability projections both with and without training costs, OpenAI and Anthropic are essentially arguing that training should be viewed as an investment (like R&D) rather than an operating expense. This framing makes the business look closer to profitability but also raises questions about whether the companies can ever reduce training spending without falling behind competitors.

Industry Impact

The inference cost revelation is likely to accelerate several industry trends. Investment in inference-optimised hardware — including custom AI chips from Google (TPUs), Amazon (Trainium and Inferentia), Microsoft (Maia), and a wave of startups — will intensify as companies seek to reduce their dependency on expensive Nvidia GPUs. Every dollar saved on inference hardware flows directly to the bottom line.

Model efficiency research will also receive increased attention. Techniques like model distillation (creating smaller models that approximate larger ones), quantisation (reducing the precision of model calculations), and speculative decoding (using small models to speed up large model inference) all promise to reduce inference costs. The companies that achieve the best cost-performance ratios will have significant competitive advantages.

For the broader technology industry, the AI cost structure creates opportunities for companies that can offer AI capabilities more efficiently. Open-source models running on optimised infrastructure may offer enterprises a more cost-effective alternative to commercial AI APIs, particularly for high-volume use cases where per-query costs compound quickly.

The implications extend to technology purchasing decisions across the board. Organisations investing in genuine Windows 11 key deployments and productivity infrastructure should consider how AI cost structures might affect the pricing and availability of AI-enhanced features in the tools they depend on.

Expert Perspective

Technology industry analysts have long warned that the AI industry's economics are not sustainable at current pricing levels. The inference cost data confirms what many suspected: AI companies are effectively subsidising usage with investor capital, keeping prices artificially low to drive adoption while hoping that scale, efficiency improvements, and hardware cost reductions will eventually make the business model work. This 'grow now, profit later' strategy is familiar from the early days of cloud computing and ride-sharing, but the capital intensity of AI infrastructure makes the path to profitability more challenging.

Financial analysts note that the accounting treatment of training costs is particularly important for potential IPOs. If OpenAI proceeds with its planned public offering, how it classifies training costs — as operating expenses or capitalised investments — will significantly affect how investors perceive the company's financial health and growth trajectory.

What This Means for Businesses

Businesses relying on commercial AI services should plan for potential price increases as AI providers move toward sustainable economics. Diversifying AI tooling across multiple providers, evaluating open-source alternatives for high-volume use cases, and monitoring vendor financial health are all prudent strategies. Organisations should also negotiate multi-year pricing commitments where possible to protect against near-term price volatility.

For businesses evaluating their technology spend on enterprise productivity software and AI services, understanding the underlying economics helps inform smarter purchasing decisions. AI features bundled into existing software subscriptions may represent better value than standalone AI services, as the costs are amortised across larger user bases and revenue streams.

Looking Ahead

The next 18-24 months will be critical for AI industry economics. Advances in inference hardware, model efficiency, and deployment optimisation will determine whether AI companies can achieve the margins needed to justify their valuations. OpenAI's potential IPO will bring unprecedented transparency to AI economics, and the resulting scrutiny may reshape investor expectations across the entire sector. Meanwhile, enterprises should treat AI cost management as a strategic priority, building flexibility into their technology architectures to adapt as the economic landscape evolves.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are AI inference costs so high?

Every AI query requires real-time computation on expensive GPU hardware. Unlike traditional software where serving additional users costs almost nothing, each AI interaction consumes significant compute resources that scale linearly with usage.

Will AI services get more expensive?

It's possible. Current pricing may be subsidised by investor capital to drive adoption. As AI companies move toward profitability, price increases are likely unless hardware costs drop significantly or model efficiency improves dramatically.

How can businesses manage AI costs?

Diversify across multiple AI providers, evaluate open-source alternatives for high-volume use cases, negotiate multi-year pricing commitments, and consider AI features bundled into existing software subscriptions rather than standalone services.

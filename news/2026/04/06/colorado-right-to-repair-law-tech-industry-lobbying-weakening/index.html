Tech Industry Lobbying Threatens to Gut Colorado's Landmark Right-to-Repair Law

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-04-06

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Tech companies are lobbying to weaken Colorado's pioneering right-to-repair law through new amendments
  • Proposed changes would exempt many devices and restrict access to diagnostic tools and parts
  • The outcome will set precedent for right-to-repair legislation across the United States
  • Independent repair shops and environmental sustainability are directly at stake

What Happened

Major technology companies are actively working to weaken Colorado's pioneering right-to-repair legislation through a new state bill that would effectively strip consumers and independent repair shops of the protections the original law was designed to provide. According to a detailed investigation by Ars Technica, industry lobbyists have crafted amendments that would exempt entire categories of devices, restrict access to diagnostic tools, and create loopholes that render the repair protections largely toothless.

Colorado's right-to-repair law, passed with broad bipartisan support, was hailed as one of the strongest consumer protection measures in the technology space. It required manufacturers to provide repair documentation, diagnostic tools, and replacement parts to consumers and independent repair shops on fair and reasonable terms. The law was seen as a template for other states considering similar legislation.

The new bill, backed by lobbying efforts from major hardware manufacturers and technology trade associations, would introduce exemptions for devices with 'embedded security features,' restrict the definition of 'fair and reasonable' pricing for parts and tools, and allow manufacturers to withhold diagnostic software under intellectual property protections. Consumer advocates argue that these provisions would effectively allow manufacturers to block most independent repairs while technically complying with the law.

Background and Context

The right-to-repair movement has been gaining momentum for over a decade, driven by consumer frustration with manufacturers who design products to be difficult or impossible to repair independently. Apple's serialisation of iPhone components, John Deere's software locks on farm equipment, and medical device manufacturers' restriction of repair information have all been flashpoints in the debate.

Colorado joined a growing list of states that have passed right-to-repair legislation, following Massachusetts, New York, California, and Minnesota. The federal government has also signalled support through executive orders directing the FTC to examine repair restrictions. The legislative trend appeared to be moving strongly in favour of repair rights, making the current lobbying effort particularly notable as an attempt to reverse course.

The technology industry has traditionally opposed right-to-repair legislation through trade associations like TechNet, the Consumer Technology Association, and CompTIA. Their arguments centre on security concerns — that providing repair access could create vulnerabilities — and safety risks — that unqualified repairs could harm consumers. However, independent repair advocates counter that these arguments are pretextual, designed to protect the lucrative manufacturer-controlled repair market rather than genuine consumer safety interests.

Why This Matters

The outcome of the Colorado fight has implications far beyond a single state. If the technology industry successfully weakens Colorado's law, it establishes a playbook for gutting similar legislation across the country. Conversely, if consumer advocates successfully defend the original protections, it reinforces the legislative model and encourages other states to pass strong repair laws.

For consumers, the practical implications are significant. Repair restrictions drive up costs, contribute to electronic waste, and force consumers into manufacturer-controlled repair ecosystems that are often more expensive and less accessible than independent alternatives. When a laptop screen cracks or a phone battery degrades, consumers should have the choice to repair the device affordably rather than being forced to purchase a replacement or pay premium manufacturer repair prices.

The issue extends to business computing as well. Organisations managing fleets of devices — from workstations running a genuine Windows 11 key to servers and networking equipment — benefit from the ability to source repairs independently. Right-to-repair protections help businesses control their IT maintenance costs and reduce dependency on manufacturer service contracts that may not align with their operational needs or budgets.

Industry Impact

The independent repair industry, estimated at over $4 billion in the United States alone, stands to be significantly affected by the outcome of the Colorado legislative battle. Independent repair shops, which provide affordable alternatives to manufacturer repair services, rely on access to parts, tools, and documentation that right-to-repair laws guarantee. Without these protections, the independent repair market faces gradual erosion as manufacturers increasingly lock down their devices.

The environmental implications are also substantial. The UN's Global E-Waste Monitor estimates that the world generates over 60 million tonnes of electronic waste annually, with less than 20% being formally recycled. Repair-friendly policies that extend device lifespans are a critical component of reducing e-waste. Weakening right-to-repair laws works against global sustainability goals and the growing consumer preference for environmentally responsible technology practices.

For the broader technology market, the repair debate intersects with growing concerns about planned obsolescence and subscription-based business models. Consumers are increasingly aware that hardware manufacturers design products with limited repairability to drive upgrade cycles. Right-to-repair legislation pushes back against this trend, and the industry's lobbying response reveals how central planned obsolescence is to current business models.

Businesses investing in their technology infrastructure, including affordable Microsoft Office licence deployments and hardware purchases, benefit from a competitive repair market that keeps maintenance costs in check and extends the useful life of their equipment.

Expert Perspective

Consumer advocacy organisations, including iFixit, the US PIRG, and the Repair Association, have been at the forefront of defending right-to-repair laws. Their research consistently shows that manufacturer claims about security and safety risks from independent repair are not supported by evidence. Independent repair shops have operated safely for decades, and the security concerns raised by manufacturers can be addressed through reasonable safeguards that don't require blocking repair access entirely.

Legal scholars specialising in technology policy note that the Colorado lobbying effort follows a familiar pattern: the industry initially opposes legislation, then — when public support makes outright opposition untenable — shifts to a strategy of accepting the legislation in principle while working to weaken it through amendments and exemptions. This approach is often more effective than outright opposition because it receives less public attention.

What This Means for Businesses

Businesses that rely on independent repair services for their technology equipment should pay attention to the Colorado situation and similar legislative battles in their own states. Supporting right-to-repair legislation — through industry associations, direct advocacy, or public comment processes — helps protect the competitive repair market that keeps business IT costs manageable.

For organisations evaluating their enterprise productivity software and hardware strategies, factoring repairability into purchasing decisions is increasingly important. Choosing devices and platforms that support independent repair reduces long-term total cost of ownership and provides more flexibility in managing technology assets.

  • The outcome will influence right-to-repair legislation across the United States
  • Consumer advocates argue the amendments would make the law effectively unenforceable
  • The independent repair industry and environmental sustainability are directly at stake
  • Businesses benefit from strong right-to-repair protections that keep maintenance costs competitive

Looking Ahead

The Colorado legislative fight will likely come to a head in the current session, with both sides mobilising advocates and resources. The outcome will set an important precedent for the dozens of other states considering right-to-repair legislation. At the federal level, the FTC continues to examine repair restrictions, and bipartisan support for repair rights suggests that national legislation may eventually supplement state-level protections. The technology industry's strategy of accepting repair laws while weakening them from within will face increasing scrutiny as consumer awareness of the issue grows.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Colorado's right-to-repair law?

Colorado's law requires manufacturers to provide repair documentation, diagnostic tools, and replacement parts to consumers and independent repair shops on fair and reasonable terms, giving device owners the right to repair their own equipment.

How would the new bill change the law?

The proposed amendments would exempt devices with 'embedded security features,' restrict the definition of fair pricing for parts, and allow manufacturers to withhold diagnostic software under intellectual property claims — effectively creating loopholes that undermine repair access.

Why does right-to-repair matter for businesses?

Businesses managing technology fleets benefit from competitive repair markets that keep maintenance costs lower than manufacturer-controlled alternatives. Right-to-repair laws also extend device useful life, reducing capital expenditure on replacements.

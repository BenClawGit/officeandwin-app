Orbital Wants to Put AI Data Centers in Space to Solve Earth's Power and Cooling Crisis

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-04-14

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Startup Orbital is proposing to build solar-powered AI compute satellites that would operate in orbit, eliminating the power and cooling constraints facing terrestrial data centers
  • Building gigawatt-scale data centers on Earth is becoming increasingly difficult due to grid capacity limits, water usage restrictions, and community opposition
  • Space-based infrastructure could theoretically access unlimited solar power and use the vacuum of space for passive cooling
  • The concept faces enormous technical, regulatory, and economic challenges, but the underlying problem it addresses is real and growing

A startup called Orbital has outlined a vision for space-based AI infrastructure that would place compute satellites in orbit, powered by solar arrays and cooled by the vacuum of space. The proposal, detailed in a TechRadar report, addresses a genuine and growing problem: building gigawatt-scale AI data centers on Earth is becoming increasingly difficult due to power grid capacity limitations, water usage restrictions for cooling, and community opposition to massive industrial facilities.

The scale of the power problem is staggering. A single large AI training run can consume as much electricity as a small city. Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang has projected that AI infrastructure will require tens of gigawatts of additional power generation in the coming decade. Finding locations with sufficient grid capacity, affordable land, and community acceptance for facilities of this scale is a growing challenge, particularly in the United States and Europe where permitting processes can take years.

Orbital's concept leverages two advantages of the space environment. First, unlimited solar power: satellites in orbit receive constant sunlight without atmospheric interference, and can deploy large solar arrays without worrying about land use, cloud cover, or day-night cycles. Second, passive cooling: the vacuum of space provides an infinite heat sink, eliminating the need for the enormous water and energy consumption that terrestrial data center cooling systems require.

The technical challenges, however, are equally enormous. Launching data center hardware into orbit costs thousands of dollars per kilogram, and current AI servers are not designed for the radiation, thermal cycling, and vibration environments of space. Maintenance is impossible — failed components cannot be replaced. Network latency between Earth and orbit adds 20-40 milliseconds per round trip, which is acceptable for batch processing but problematic for real-time inference. And the regulatory framework for orbital data centers does not exist.

For organizations running Microsoft Azure or other cloud infrastructure, the relevance is indirect but real. The power and cooling constraints driving Orbital's proposal affect all cloud providers. Microsoft, Google, and Amazon are all investing in nuclear power, advanced cooling technologies, and new data center locations to address these constraints. If terrestrial solutions cannot keep pace with AI compute demand, unconventional approaches — including space-based infrastructure — will receive more attention and investment.

The most likely near-term outcome is not orbital data centers but rather a diversification of terrestrial solutions: modular nuclear reactors, underwater data centers (Microsoft's Project Natick explored this), high-altitude balloon platforms, and advanced closed-loop cooling systems. Orbital's vision may eventually become technically feasible, but the timeline is likely measured in decades rather than years.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Orbital proposing?

Building solar-powered AI compute satellites in orbit that use the vacuum of space for passive cooling, eliminating the power and cooling constraints of terrestrial data centers.

Why is this being considered?

Building gigawatt-scale AI data centers on Earth faces grid capacity limits, water restrictions, permitting delays, and community opposition. The problem is real and growing rapidly.

What are the advantages of space-based data centers?

Unlimited solar power without atmospheric interference, passive cooling from the vacuum of space, no land use conflicts, and no water consumption for cooling.

What are the main challenges?

Extremely high launch costs, radiation and thermal cycling damage to hardware, impossible maintenance, network latency of 20-40ms, and no regulatory framework for orbital data centers.

When could this become reality?

Likely decades away. More near-term solutions include modular nuclear reactors, underwater data centers, and advanced closed-loop cooling systems. Microsoft's Project Natick already tested underwater data centers.

How does this affect cloud computing users?

Indirectly. Power and cooling constraints affect all cloud providers and may eventually limit compute availability or increase costs. Microsoft, Google, and Amazon are investing in nuclear power and advanced cooling to address these constraints.

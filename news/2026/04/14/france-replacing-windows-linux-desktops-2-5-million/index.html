France Is Replacing 2.5 Million Windows Desktops With Linux — Here Is What the New Stack Looks Like

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-04-14

⚡ Quick Summary

  • France has announced plans to replace 2.5 million government Windows desktops with a Linux-based operating system as part of a digital sovereignty initiative
  • The migration represents one of the largest government-to-Linux desktop transitions ever attempted, dwarfing previous efforts by Munich and other municipalities
  • The new Linux stack reportedly includes a customized GNOME desktop, LibreOffice for productivity, and Thunderbird for email
  • The transition raises questions about application compatibility, user training costs, and whether the savings from eliminating Windows licenses justify the migration effort

France has officially committed to replacing approximately 2.5 million Windows desktops across its government agencies with a Linux-based operating system, marking one of the most ambitious government-to-Linux desktop migrations ever attempted. The initiative, driven by digital sovereignty concerns and the desire to reduce dependence on American technology companies, would reshape the technology infrastructure of French public administration at a scale that previous Linux desktop efforts never achieved.

The planned stack, according to sources and expert analysis, centers on a customized Linux distribution with a GNOME desktop environment tailored for government workflows. LibreOffice would replace Microsoft Office for document creation and editing, Thunderbird would handle email, and a curated set of open source applications would cover the majority of standard government computing needs. For specialized applications that lack open source equivalents, compatibility layers and virtualization solutions would provide access to Windows-only software.

The scale of this migration is unprecedented. Previous government Linux desktop projects — notably Munich's LiMux initiative, which ran from 2003 to 2020 — involved tens of thousands of desktops and faced significant challenges including application compatibility, user resistance, and higher-than-expected support costs. Munich ultimately returned to Windows. France's project is roughly 100 times larger, which amplifies both the potential savings and the potential problems.

On the savings side, eliminating Windows licenses across 2.5 million devices represents substantial cost avoidance. At enterprise volume licensing rates, even a modest per-device Windows license fee multiplied by 2.5 million machines amounts to hundreds of millions of euros over a typical refresh cycle. Office licenses compound these savings further.

On the cost side, the migration requires extensive user training, application compatibility testing, custom development for the Linux distribution, and a substantially larger IT support operation during and after the transition. Government employees who have used Windows and Microsoft Office for their entire careers face a significant learning curve with Linux and LibreOffice, and productivity losses during the transition period are real and measurable.

The digital sovereignty argument is politically powerful in France and across the European Union. Dependence on American technology companies for critical government infrastructure has been framed as a strategic vulnerability, and the ongoing geopolitical tensions have strengthened the case for reducing this dependence. Whether the practical challenges of the migration can be overcome remains to be seen, but France's commitment signals a serious and sustained effort rather than a symbolic gesture.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many desktops is France replacing?

Approximately 2.5 million government Windows desktops will be replaced with Linux as part of a digital sovereignty initiative.

What Linux distribution will France use?

Details are not officially confirmed, but the stack reportedly includes a customized Linux distribution with GNOME desktop, LibreOffice, and Thunderbird for standard government workflows.

Has any government done this before?

Munich, Germany attempted a similar migration (LiMux) with 15,000 desktops from 2003-2020, but ultimately returned to Windows due to compatibility and support challenges. France's project is roughly 100x larger.

Why is France doing this?

Digital sovereignty — reducing dependence on American technology companies for critical government infrastructure. Geopolitical tensions have strengthened the political case for this transition.

What about Windows-only applications?

Compatibility layers and virtualization solutions would provide access to Windows-only software that lacks open source equivalents.

How much money could France save?

Eliminating Windows and Office licenses across 2.5 million devices could save hundreds of millions of euros. However, migration costs including training, development, and support may offset much of these savings.

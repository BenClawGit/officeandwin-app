Niobium Launches The Fog: Fully Encrypted AI Processing That Never Decrypts Your Data

Startup Niobium Microsystems has unveiled The Fog, the first cloud platform that enables organizations to run AI and data processing workloads on fully encrypted data using homomorphic encryption — meaning sensitive information never needs to be decrypted, even during processing.

What Happened

Niobium Microsystems has announced The Fog, a platform that leverages fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) to enable organizations to process their most sensitive data in cloud environments without ever decrypting it. Unlike traditional encryption that protects data at rest (stored) and in transit (moving between systems), FHE protects data while it is actively being computed upon — closing the last remaining gap in data protection.

The platform is designed to let developers run AI workloads, analytics queries, and data processing operations on encrypted datasets using standard development tools. The decryption keys remain exclusively with the data owner, ensuring that neither Niobium, the cloud provider, nor any other third party can access the underlying information — even while performing computations on it.

Niobium CEO Kevin Yoder described the platform as eliminating the tradeoff that has long forced organizations to choose between data security and data utility. "Many companies just accept the risk of data exposure as the cost of doing business in the cloud," Yoder said. "The Fog eliminates that tradeoff."

Background and Context

Fully homomorphic encryption has been a theoretical possibility since Craig Gentry's foundational work in 2009, but practical implementation has been blocked by massive computational overheads. Early FHE implementations were thousands to millions of times slower than equivalent operations on unencrypted data, making them impractical for real-world use.

The technology has advanced significantly through both algorithmic improvements and specialized hardware acceleration. IBM, Intel, Microsoft, and several startups have invested in making FHE practical, but no platform has achieved the combination of performance, developer accessibility, and commercial readiness that Niobium claims for The Fog.

The market need is acute. Organizations in healthcare, financial services, government, and defense hold vast quantities of sensitive data that could generate valuable insights through AI analysis. However, compliance regulations — including HIPAA, GDPR, PCI DSS, and various national security frameworks — restrict how and where this data can be processed. The requirement to decrypt data for processing creates a vulnerability window that security-conscious organizations find unacceptable.

Why This Matters

If The Fog delivers on its promises, it represents a paradigm shift in cloud computing security. The current model requires organizations to trust their cloud provider with decrypted data during processing — a trust requirement that has been a primary barrier to cloud adoption for highly regulated industries. FHE eliminates this trust requirement entirely.

The implications for AI specifically are profound. The most valuable datasets for AI training and inference — medical records, financial transactions, classified government information — are often the most restricted. A platform that enables AI processing on this data without decryption could unlock enormous value while maintaining strict compliance. For enterprises managing sensitive operations through genuine Windows 11 key environments and cloud deployments, FHE-capable platforms could resolve the long-standing tension between data utilization and data protection.

Industry Impact

The major cloud providers — AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud — will be watching Niobium closely. If FHE becomes commercially viable, it fundamentally changes the competitive dynamics of cloud computing. Currently, cloud providers have access to customer data during processing, which creates both security risk and regulatory complexity. A cloud environment where the provider provably cannot access customer data is a powerful competitive differentiator.

For the healthcare industry, where data sharing for research has been constrained by privacy regulations, FHE could enable multi-institutional AI research on patient data without any institution sharing decrypted records. Financial services could run fraud detection algorithms across encrypted transaction data. Defense and intelligence organizations could process classified information in commercial cloud environments.

Companies providing enterprise productivity software and cloud services should monitor FHE development closely, as it may eventually reshape expectations about data privacy in all cloud-based software, not just specialized AI workloads.

Expert Perspective

Cryptography researchers express cautious optimism about The Fog while noting that the performance claims need independent verification. The historical challenge with FHE has been the computational overhead — even with improvements, FHE operations typically take 100-1000x longer than equivalent unencrypted operations. Whether Niobium has achieved performance levels that are practical for production AI workloads remains to be demonstrated at scale.

Security architects note that even if the computational overhead remains significant, there are high-value use cases where the security benefit justifies the performance cost. Processing a month's worth of encrypted medical records to train an AI model doesn't need to happen in real-time — it just needs to happen securely.

What This Means for Businesses

Organizations sitting on sensitive data that could benefit from AI analysis should evaluate whether The Fog or similar FHE platforms can unlock insights they've been unable to pursue due to compliance constraints. The first movers in FHE-enabled AI analysis may gain significant competitive advantages in data-driven industries.

For businesses using affordable Microsoft Office licence deployments alongside cloud services, the evolution of encrypted computing may eventually enable new levels of data privacy for everyday business operations, not just specialized AI workloads.

The Fog represents the beginning of what could become a new category of cloud computing: provably private computing. If Niobium demonstrates that FHE can perform at commercially viable speeds for AI workloads, expect every major cloud provider to develop competing capabilities. The long-term vision — a cloud where data owners never need to trust their computing provider with decrypted data — would resolve one of the foundational concerns that has slowed cloud adoption in the most sensitive industries.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is fully homomorphic encryption?

FHE is a cryptographic technique that enables computations to be performed on encrypted data without decrypting it first. The results, when decrypted, match what would have been produced by operating on the unencrypted data.

Why hasn't FHE been used before?

FHE has been theoretically possible since 2009 but was impractical due to massive computational overhead — operations took thousands to millions of times longer than on unencrypted data. Algorithmic improvements and hardware acceleration have progressively reduced this gap.

Which industries benefit most from encrypted AI processing?

Healthcare, financial services, government, and defense sectors hold the most sensitive data with the strictest compliance requirements. FHE could unlock AI analysis of data that has been impossible to process in cloud environments due to privacy regulations.

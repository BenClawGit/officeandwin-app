India's Smartphone Export Surge Hits $11B — and the Iran War Could Erase a Quarter of It

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-31

⚡ Quick Summary

  • India's smartphone exports reached $11B in H1 FY2026 (+55% YoY), driven by Apple and Samsung manufacturing expansion
  • The Iran conflict threatens shipping lanes for component supply chains, with analysts warning of 22-25% export volume drops
  • India assembles at world-class scale but remains dependent on imported components, creating structural supply chain vulnerability
  • Enterprise IT teams should factor hardware supply disruption scenarios into 2026 procurement planning

What Happened

India's smartphone export sector has reached a landmark milestone, with official government data showing exports of $11 billion in the first half of FY2026 — a 55% increase year-on-year that cements India's position as the world's second-largest smartphone manufacturer after China. The growth has been driven primarily by Apple's continued manufacturing shift to India via its contract manufacturing partners Tata Electronics and Foxconn, with Samsung's Indian operations also contributing significantly to the export surge.

However, the record achievement is now shadowed by a significant geopolitical risk. Analysts covering the smartphone supply chain warn that the ongoing Iran war — which has disrupted shipping lanes through the Persian Gulf and Strait of Hormuz — could trigger a 22% to 25% drop in India's smartphone export volumes in the coming weeks, as key component supply routes are disrupted and major buyers in Europe and the Middle East face delivery uncertainty. The warning comes from Nikkei Asia's analysis of shipping data, component procurement records, and buyer communications across the major smartphone contract manufacturing ecosystem.

The convergence of record export growth and acute supply chain vulnerability illustrates a central tension in India's electronics manufacturing ambition: the country has made enormous strides in building final assembly capacity, but remains deeply dependent on component supply chains that flow through geopolitically sensitive regions — particularly the sea lanes connecting Southeast Asian component manufacturers to Indian assembly facilities.

Background and Context

India's smartphone manufacturing transformation over the past five years has been one of the most consequential industrial policy achievements in recent memory. The Indian government's Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme for mobile electronics, introduced in 2020, offered substantial cash incentives to manufacturers who expanded production in India — and the scheme's results have exceeded most projections. Apple's manufacturing shift has been particularly dramatic: in 2021, India accounted for roughly 1% of global iPhone production; by 2025, that figure had grown to approximately 14-15%, and Apple has publicly committed to further expanding Indian manufacturing capacity.

The strategic rationale for diversifying iPhone production away from China — Apple's dominant manufacturing base for three decades — has been well-documented: US-China trade tensions, concerns about concentration risk following COVID-era supply disruptions, and growing US government pressure on companies to reduce dependence on Chinese manufacturing have all contributed to Apple's India push. India offers a combination of large manufacturing workforce, improving infrastructure, government incentives, and political alignment with Western partners that makes it an attractive alternative to China for technology supply chains.

Samsung's parallel investment in Indian manufacturing is driven by similar logic, augmented by Samsung's existing strong market position in India's domestic smartphone market. The Korean giant has been producing smartphones in India since 2007 and has steadily upgraded its Indian facilities to handle export-quality production at scale.

The geopolitical risk from the Iran conflict is a reminder that supply chain diversification from China to India does not eliminate supply chain concentration risk — it shifts it to a different geographic and geopolitical context. India's component supply chains run through the Indian Ocean, Red Sea, and Persian Gulf — shipping lanes that are directly affected by conflict in the Middle East. What appeared as a diversification is now revealed as a different kind of concentration vulnerability.

Why This Matters

The smartphone industry is among the most globally integrated manufacturing sectors in the world. A flagship smartphone contains components from dozens of countries — display panels from South Korea, memory chips from Taiwan and South Korea, camera modules from Japan and Taiwan, processors designed in the UK and US and manufactured in Taiwan, and hundreds of smaller components from across Southeast Asia and China. Final assembly in India represents the last step in a supply chain that stretches across virtually every major technology manufacturing nation.

When any link in that chain is disrupted — whether by pandemic, trade policy, or armed conflict — the effects ripple across the entire ecosystem. A 22-25% drop in India's smartphone export volumes would represent an approximately $2.5-3 billion reduction in the current run-rate, affecting not just Indian manufacturers and government export revenue projections but also the global availability and pricing of smartphones in key markets.

For consumers and businesses purchasing smartphones and related hardware, supply disruptions typically manifest as price increases and longer delivery times before they result in outright unavailability. The smartphone market, which has been characterised by strong competition and stable pricing for the past two to three years, could see meaningful price pressure if the supply chain disruption is severe or prolonged.

Industry Impact

Apple is the most exposed of the major smartphone brands to the India supply chain vulnerability, given the pace at which it has shifted production to India over the past four years. If Indian iPhone production faces component shortages due to shipping disruptions, Apple will need to evaluate whether it can absorb the cost of rerouting component deliveries through alternative shipping lanes — longer routes via the Cape of Good Hope, for example — or accelerating air freight to maintain production volumes.

Air freight significantly increases component logistics costs — roughly 10-20 times the cost per unit of sea freight — and is not economically viable at scale for all but the highest-value components. Apple's high device prices and margins give it more latitude to absorb air freight premiums than lower-cost Android manufacturers, but even for Apple, sustained air freight dependency would be a meaningful margin headwind.

Samsung and Chinese Android manufacturers like Xiaomi, OPPO, and Vivo, which produce at lower margins than Apple, have less room to absorb elevated logistics costs. These manufacturers may respond to supply constraints by reducing export volumes more aggressively, prioritising their most profitable product lines, or seeking component alternatives from suppliers with different supply routes.

The broader supply chain resilience conversation will intensify as the Iran conflict's effects on shipping become clearer. Technology companies and their investors have been investing heavily in supply chain risk management since the COVID-era disruptions of 2020-2021; the Iran-related shipping risk is a test of how much that investment has improved actual resilience versus simply improved documentation of existing vulnerabilities.

Expert Perspective

Supply chain analysts who specialise in electronics manufacturing have noted that India's rapid emergence as a smartphone export powerhouse has happened faster than the supporting component ecosystem has been able to develop domestically. India assembles smartphones at world-class volumes, but the components being assembled are almost entirely imported — from displays to chips to mechanical components. This import dependence is structurally different from the situation in China, where decades of manufacturing investment have built a dense local component ecosystem that reduces exposure to international shipping disruptions.

Building a local component ecosystem in India comparable to China's is a multi-decade project, not a multi-year one. In the interim, India's smartphone manufacturing success story remains geographically vulnerable to the same kinds of shipping disruptions that affected Chinese manufacturing during the COVID period — a vulnerability that the Iran conflict is now stress-testing in real time.

Geopolitical risk analysts point out that the Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of global oil trade passes and which sits at the centre of Iran-related shipping risk — is also a critical route for electronic component trade between Southeast Asian manufacturers and Indian assembly facilities. The concentration of multiple supply chain risks at a single geographic chokepoint is a systemic vulnerability that the technology industry has historically underweighted in its supply chain planning.

What This Means for Businesses

Businesses with significant hardware procurement cycles — including those purchasing large volumes of smartphones, tablets, or other consumer electronics — should build supply chain disruption scenarios into their 2026 procurement planning. Price increases and extended lead times are the most likely near-term effects of Iran-related shipping disruptions; organisations that can flex procurement timing or build modest inventory buffers will be better positioned than those with just-in-time procurement models.

IT procurement teams should communicate current supply chain risk assessments to finance and operations stakeholders who may be assuming that hardware procurement will proceed on normal timelines and pricing. Supply chain transparency — understanding not just primary suppliers but the geographic exposure of component supply chains — is increasingly a baseline competency for enterprise IT procurement.

For businesses managing mixed hardware and software environments — including those running affordable Microsoft Office licence deployments alongside mobile device management programs — the potential impact on smartphone procurement is worth flagging to mobile device refresh planning cycles. Extending the life of existing devices through software optimisation — particularly on Windows and Android platforms — may become a cost-effective hedge against hardware price increases driven by supply chain disruption.

Key Takeaways

  • The Iran conflict threatens shipping lanes critical to India's component supply chains, with analysts warning of 22-25% export volume drops
  • India's assembly capacity has grown faster than its domestic component ecosystem, creating structural import dependency
  • Apple is most exposed to Indian supply chain risk given its rapid manufacturing shift from China to India over the past four years
  • Businesses should factor supply disruption scenarios into 2026 hardware procurement planning and communicate risk to finance stakeholders

Looking Ahead

The extent of the Iran conflict's impact on India's smartphone export trajectory will depend on the duration and geographic scope of the shipping disruption. If the conflict remains contained and shipping lanes reopen within weeks, the $11 billion export run-rate may prove resilient. If the disruption extends into months, the 22-25% drop predicted by analysts could materialise, affecting both India's manufacturing growth story and global smartphone supply. Either way, the episode will accelerate discussions in the Indian government and among major electronics manufacturers about the necessity of building deeper domestic component supply chains — conversations that will shape India's industrial policy priorities for the next decade. The record export numbers of H1 FY2026 should be celebrated; the supply chain vulnerability they reveal should be taken equally seriously.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why has India's smartphone manufacturing grown so quickly?

India's Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme for mobile electronics, combined with Apple's strategic push to diversify manufacturing away from China, drove the rapid expansion. Apple's partners Tata Electronics and Foxconn have built large-scale iPhone assembly facilities in Tamil Nadu and other Indian states, while Samsung expanded its existing Indian operations to handle export production.

How does the Iran conflict affect smartphone supply chains?

Key component supply routes from Southeast Asian manufacturers to Indian assembly facilities pass through the Indian Ocean, Red Sea, and Persian Gulf — shipping lanes affected by Iran-related military activity. Disruptions to these routes force longer alternative shipping paths (adding cost and time) or expensive air freight, both of which squeeze manufacturing economics.

Will smartphone prices increase due to India supply chain disruptions?

Supply chain disruptions typically manifest as price increases and longer delivery times before causing outright shortages. The severity depends on the disruption's duration. Short disruptions may be absorbed through inventory buffers and premium logistics; extended disruptions could drive meaningful price increases, particularly for mid-range Android devices with lower margin buffers than Apple's premium products.

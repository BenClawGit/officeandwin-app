Iran-Linked Hackers Target Microsoft 365 Accounts with Password-Spray Attacks on Critical Infrastructure

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-31

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Iran-linked threat actors conducting password-spray attacks on Microsoft 365 accounts
  • Hundreds of organisations targeted, concentrated among Middle Eastern municipalities
  • Attack pattern correlates with areas affected by Iranian missile strikes
  • Legacy authentication protocols in M365 tenants remain the primary exploit vector

What Happened

Security researchers have identified a sustained password-spraying campaign conducted by suspected Iran-linked threat actors targeting hundreds of organisations, with a particular concentration on Middle Eastern municipalities and government entities. The attacks, which are focused on Microsoft 365 accounts, have been linked by researchers to operational intelligence gathering that may be supporting Iranian military assessment activities following recent regional missile strikes.

Password spraying — the technique of attempting a small number of commonly used passwords against a large number of accounts to avoid triggering lockout thresholds — has proven particularly effective against Microsoft 365 deployments where legacy authentication protocols remain enabled. The attackers appear to be methodically working through target lists compiled from open-source intelligence and leaked credential databases, attempting access to M365 accounts with minimal per-account interaction to stay below detection thresholds.

Researchers noted a significant correlation between the geographic distribution of targeted organisations and cities that have been subjects of Iranian military activity, suggesting the campaign may have an intelligence collection component rather than purely financial or disruptive motivations.

Microsoft has been notified of the campaign and is working with affected organisations to strengthen account security and disable vulnerable authentication pathways across M365 tenants.

Background and Context

Iran-linked cyberattack groups — including those tracked under designations such as APT33, APT34, and Phosphorus — have a documented history of targeting Microsoft 365 and Azure Active Directory environments as part of broader intelligence collection operations. The Microsoft 365 platform is particularly attractive to nation-state actors because it centralises enormous volumes of sensitive communications, documents, calendars, and collaboration data in a single, internet-accessible service.

Password spraying against M365 has been a recurring attack pattern for years, but it remains effective because many organisations have not fully disabled legacy authentication protocols (Basic Authentication) or implemented multi-factor authentication across all user accounts. Microsoft deprecated Basic Authentication for Exchange Online in 2022, but a significant number of tenants still have exceptions or legacy connectors that create exploitable gaps.

The geopolitical context of this campaign is significant. Security researchers' observation that target geography correlates with areas affected by missile strikes positions this as a potential bomb-damage assessment operation — using compromised email and document access to gather intelligence on the impact and aftermath of kinetic military actions. This represents a sophisticated fusion of physical and cyber intelligence operations that has become a hallmark of advanced nation-state actors.

Organisations running affordable Microsoft Office licences with genuine M365 subscriptions have access to Microsoft's built-in Conditional Access and Identity Protection features — tools that provide significant protection against password spraying when properly configured.

Why This Matters

Nation-state password-spraying campaigns against cloud productivity platforms represent a systemic risk to organisations beyond the immediate targets. When attackers successfully compromise M365 accounts belonging to municipal governments or critical infrastructure operators, the potential downstream consequences extend well beyond data theft — compromised accounts can be used to pivot into partner organisation networks, manipulate procurement processes, or conduct influence operations using trusted institutional identities.

The specific targeting of government and municipal entities also raises concerns about the resilience of cloud-first digital government initiatives. Across the Middle East, Europe, and North America, municipal governments have aggressively migrated to Microsoft 365 as a cloud productivity backbone over the past five years. This concentration of sensitive administrative data on a single platform creates a high-value target profile that will continue to attract sophisticated threat actors.

For enterprise security teams, this campaign is a reminder that cloud identity security — not perimeter network security — is the front line of modern defence. The attack does not require bypassing firewalls or exploiting network vulnerabilities; it simply requires a valid username and a weak or reused password.

Industry Impact

The campaign will accelerate enterprise and government adoption of phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication (MFA) standards, particularly FIDO2 hardware security keys and passkeys, which render password spraying attacks completely ineffective regardless of credential compromise.

Microsoft has been pushing Entra ID (formerly Azure Active Directory) conditional access policies and security defaults aggressively, and incidents like this provide concrete justification for organisations that have been slow to enforce these protections. Security vendors offering M365 posture management and identity threat detection are likely to see increased demand as organisations respond to the campaign.

For enterprise productivity software resellers and IT service providers, the incident creates an advisory opportunity — customers running M365 should be proactively engaged with security hardening recommendations, legacy authentication audits, and MFA rollout assistance.

Expert Perspective

Threat intelligence researchers emphasised that the observed correlation between attack targets and missile strike locations is not definitive proof of Iranian state involvement but represents a compelling pattern. "The operational tempo and target selection logic are consistent with a state-directed intelligence collection mission," one senior threat researcher noted. "Commercial cybercriminals don't generally care which specific municipalities they compromise; this level of geographic precision is a nation-state signature."

Microsoft's security team has documented Iranian threat actors' long-standing interest in M365 environments and noted that this campaign's password-spraying methodology is consistent with previously observed Iranian threat actor tactics. The low-and-slow approach — few password attempts per account, spread across thousands of targets — is specifically designed to avoid detection by standard account lockout policies.

What This Means for Businesses

Every organisation running Microsoft 365 should treat this campaign as a trigger for immediate security posture review. The specific mitigations are well-documented: disable legacy authentication protocols, enforce MFA across all accounts, implement Conditional Access policies that restrict sign-in from high-risk locations, and enable Microsoft's Identity Protection risk-based sign-in policies.

IT teams should audit M365 audit logs for signs of password spraying — specifically, looking for patterns of failed sign-in attempts across multiple accounts from the same IP ranges over short time windows. Microsoft Sentinel and Defender for Identity provide automated detection capabilities for this pattern. Ensuring your organisation operates on genuine Windows 11 keys and fully licensed Microsoft security tools ensures access to the complete Defender threat intelligence stack.

Key Takeaways

  • Suspected Iran-linked attackers are conducting password-spraying campaigns against Microsoft 365 accounts globally
  • Targets are concentrated among Middle Eastern municipalities and government organisations
  • Geographic correlation with missile strike locations suggests intelligence collection motivation
  • Attacks exploit legacy authentication protocols still active in many M365 tenants
  • Mitigations include disabling legacy auth, enforcing MFA, and implementing Conditional Access policies
  • Microsoft has been notified and is actively working with affected organisations

Looking Ahead

The intersection of geopolitical conflict and cyber operations will continue to intensify, and cloud productivity platforms like Microsoft 365 will remain high-value targets for nation-state intelligence collection. Organisations — particularly those in sectors relevant to regional conflicts — should assume persistent targeting and invest accordingly in identity-centric security architecture. The shift to phishing-resistant authentication standards is no longer a best practice recommendation; it is an operational necessity for any organisation operating in a threat-elevated environment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is password spraying?

Password spraying is an attack technique where attackers attempt a small number of commonly used passwords against a large number of accounts. Unlike brute force attacks, it avoids triggering account lockout policies by staying below the per-account failed login threshold.

Why is Microsoft 365 targeted by nation-state actors?

Microsoft 365 centralises enormous volumes of sensitive communications, documents, and collaboration data in a single internet-accessible service. Compromising M365 accounts gives attackers access to email, files, calendars, and internal communications without requiring network-level intrusion.

How can organisations protect their M365 accounts from password spraying?

Key mitigations include disabling legacy authentication protocols, enforcing multi-factor authentication on all accounts, implementing Conditional Access policies, and enabling Microsoft Identity Protection for risk-based sign-in controls.

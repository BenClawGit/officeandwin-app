Samsung Hearapy App Claims to Reduce Motion Sickness Using Sound — What the Science Says

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-31

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Samsung launches Hearapy — a free app claiming to reduce motion sickness via a 100Hz audio tone
  • Compatible with Galaxy Buds and other Android headphones; works via vestibulo-auditory interaction
  • Clinical validation is pending; medical community recommends treating it as a wellness tool
  • Signals Samsung's intent to expand Galaxy Buds into health technology beyond audio

What Happened

Samsung has released a new free Android application called Hearapy, now available through the Google Play Store, that claims to reduce the symptoms of motion sickness through the delivery of a low-frequency audio tone. The app, compatible with Samsung Galaxy Buds and other connected headphones, plays a continuous 100Hz sine wave tone for 60 seconds when activated, with Samsung asserting that this auditory stimulation can alleviate the nausea, dizziness, and disorientation associated with motion sickness.

The application is straightforward in design. Users experiencing motion sickness symptoms open Hearapy, connect their compatible headphones, and activate the 60-second tone delivery. Samsung's promotional materials describe the mechanism as working through the auditory system's influence on the vestibular system — the inner ear mechanism responsible for balance and spatial orientation that, when disrupted, produces motion sickness symptoms.

Hearapy is available at no cost and does not require a Samsung Galaxy device to download, though certain features may be optimised for Galaxy Buds' audio hardware. The app launched alongside broader Samsung health and wellness initiatives and represents Samsung's move into audio-based health intervention, a nascent but rapidly growing category.

Background and Context

Motion sickness is a significant and underserved health problem. Estimates suggest that approximately one-third of the global population is highly susceptible to motion sickness, with additional segments experiencing symptoms under specific conditions — car travel, air travel, virtual reality, or reading while in motion. The condition disproportionately affects women and children and has significant economic implications: it is a major barrier to adoption of VR technology, limits productivity for commuters who work during travel, and affects quality of life for hundreds of millions of people.

Existing interventions — anti-nausea medication (dimenhydrinate, scopolamine patches), pressure point wristbands, and behavioural strategies (fixing gaze on the horizon, positioning near the front of vehicles) — are either pharmacological, with associated side effects, or inconsistently effective. A non-pharmaceutical, wearable audio-based intervention addresses a genuine gap in the consumer health market.

The scientific basis for audio-based motion sickness relief is early-stage but not without foundation. Research into the vestibulo-auditory interaction — the neurological relationship between the inner ear's balance system and its hearing system — suggests that specific frequency stimulation through bone conduction or air conduction hearing may modulate vestibular processing. Samsung has not published peer-reviewed clinical trial data to support Hearapy's efficacy claims, which has drawn both interest and scepticism from the medical community.

The launch also positions Samsung's Galaxy Buds ecosystem as a health platform, not merely an audio product — a strategic expansion that parallels Apple's positioning of AirPods as hearing health tools and that complements Samsung's Galaxy Watch health monitoring capabilities.

Why This Matters

If Hearapy's claimed efficacy is validated by independent research, it would represent a meaningful advance in consumer health technology with a large addressable market. Motion sickness treatment has attracted very little pharmaceutical innovation in recent decades, making a non-pharmaceutical digital health intervention particularly significant.

From a technology market perspective, the app signals Samsung's intent to expand beyond hardware into health services delivered through its device ecosystem. This mirrors a broader trend across consumer electronics: Apple Health, Google Fit, and Samsung Health have progressively added clinical-grade monitoring and intervention capabilities, blurring the boundary between consumer electronics and medical devices.

The timing is also significant for the VR industry. Motion sickness is one of the primary barriers to extended VR headset use, and a consumer-grade audio intervention that reduces symptoms could meaningfully expand the VR market's accessible user base. Companies developing VR platforms — and the genuine Windows 11 platform increasingly supports VR and mixed reality experiences — have a vested interest in whether interventions like Hearapy prove effective at scale.

Industry Impact

The consumer health audio space will watch Hearapy's reception closely. If Samsung generates compelling real-world user data — even anecdotal at scale — it will accelerate investment and development in audio-based health interventions from other wearable and headphone manufacturers. Apple, Sony, Jabra, and Bose all have the audio hardware ecosystem to deploy similar features; Hearapy may be the proof of concept that triggers broader industry exploration.

For the pharmaceutical industry, a non-pharmaceutical competitor in the motion sickness treatment space is unlikely to represent a material commercial threat in the near term, given the limited clinical evidence for audio-based interventions. However, if clinical validation follows, the competitive dynamics could shift meaningfully for over-the-counter motion sickness remedy sales.

Samsung's integration of Hearapy into its broader enterprise productivity software ecosystem may also have workplace health applications — for teams that rely on transport for field work, or for employees who experience VR-related nausea in training and simulation environments.

Expert Perspective

Audiologists and vestibular researchers offered cautious assessments of Hearapy's claims. "The vestibulo-auditory connection is real and has been studied, but the evidence for a specific 100Hz tone as a clinically effective motion sickness intervention is not yet established," one vestibular specialist noted. "Samsung releasing this as a free wellness app, rather than as a medical device claim, is the appropriate positioning given the current evidence base."

User experience researchers were more optimistic about the app's potential: "Even if the mechanism is partially placebo-mediated, a free, zero-side-effect intervention that some users find helpful is a net positive. The key question is whether Samsung will invest in rigorous clinical research to establish efficacy parameters."

What This Means for Businesses

For employers and HR teams, Hearapy-style health interventions represent a category worth monitoring as part of a broader digital employee wellness toolkit. Motion sickness-related productivity loss among commuting and travelling employees is a real but rarely quantified cost; accessible digital interventions could reduce it without pharmacological intervention.

Technology procurement teams evaluating Samsung hardware for enterprise deployments should note the growing health feature set of Samsung's Galaxy ecosystem. As health and productivity increasingly converge on a single device platform, alongside tools like affordable Microsoft Office licences for knowledge work productivity, the case for ecosystem integration strengthens.

Key Takeaways

  • Samsung has launched Hearapy, a free Android app claiming to reduce motion sickness by playing a 100Hz sine wave tone through headphones
  • The app works with Galaxy Buds and other compatible headphones and is available on Google Play
  • Scientific basis lies in vestibulo-auditory interaction research, but peer-reviewed clinical validation is pending
  • Positions Galaxy Buds ecosystem as a health platform beyond pure audio use
  • Could have significant implications for VR adoption if audio-based motion sickness relief proves effective at scale
  • Medical professionals recommend treating the app as a wellness tool, not a clinically validated medical intervention

Looking Ahead

Samsung will need to invest in independent clinical research to substantiate Hearapy's efficacy claims and differentiate the app from the category of wellness applications with unvalidated health benefits. If clinical trials support the mechanism, the app could become a significant product in Samsung Health's portfolio and a catalyst for audio-based health interventions across the industry. The category is worth watching as wearable technology continues its convergence with consumer health.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does Samsung Hearapy claim to reduce motion sickness?

Hearapy plays a 100Hz sine wave tone through connected headphones for 60 seconds. Samsung claims this works through the vestibulo-auditory connection — the neurological relationship between the inner ear's balance system and hearing — to modulate vestibular processing and reduce motion sickness symptoms.

Is there scientific evidence that sound can treat motion sickness?

Research into vestibulo-auditory interaction provides a theoretical basis for audio-based balance intervention, but peer-reviewed clinical trial evidence specifically for Hearapy's 100Hz tone mechanism has not yet been published. Independent clinical validation is needed before medical efficacy claims can be made.

Is Hearapy free and what devices does it work with?

Yes, Hearapy is free on the Google Play Store and works with Samsung Galaxy Buds and other compatible Android headphones. It does not require a Samsung Galaxy phone to download, though some features may be optimised for Samsung hardware.

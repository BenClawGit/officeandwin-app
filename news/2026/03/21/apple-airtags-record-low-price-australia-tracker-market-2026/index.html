Apple AirTags Drop to Record Low Price in Australia as Tracker Market Heats Up Ahead of Next-Gen Launch

Apple's first-generation AirTag Bluetooth trackers have hit their lowest-ever price in Australia, with Amazon dropping the four-pack to just AU$99 — making each tracker a mere AU$24.75. The aggressive pricing signals both retail clearing of current inventory and the intensifying competition in the personal item tracking market.

What Happened

Amazon Australia has reduced the price of the Apple AirTag four-pack to AU$99, the lowest price recorded since the product launched in April 2021. At AU$24.75 per tracker, the deal represents a significant discount from Apple's standard retail pricing of AU$49 per individual AirTag or AU$159 for the four-pack. The price drop applies to the first-generation AirTag, which uses a standard CR2032 battery and leverages Apple's vast Find My network of over a billion devices for location tracking.

The timing of the price reduction is notable. Industry analysts and supply chain reports have increasingly pointed to Apple preparing a second-generation AirTag, widely expected to feature improved ultra-wideband (UWB) technology, enhanced range, and potentially a speaker redesign that addresses anti-stalking concerns. Retailers typically begin clearing inventory of current-generation Apple products weeks or months before updated versions appear.

The deal is available through Amazon's standard marketplace, without requiring Prime membership or special promotional codes. However, stock at this price level is expected to be limited, as similar deals on Apple products have historically sold out quickly in the Australian market.

Background and Context

Apple launched AirTags in 2021 as its entry into the Bluetooth tracker market previously dominated by Tile. The product leveraged Apple's unique advantage — the massive installed base of iPhones, iPads, and Macs that form the Find My network — to create a tracking system with unmatched coverage. An AirTag doesn't need cellular connectivity; instead, it communicates anonymously with nearby Apple devices, which relay its location to the owner through Apple's encrypted network.

The original AirTag has remained largely unchanged since launch, a rarity in Apple's product lineup where annual updates are the norm. This extended product cycle has fueled speculation about a significant upgrade in the next generation, with rumored features including the newer Apple U2 chip for improved precision finding, a built-in speaker that's harder to disable (addressing stalking concerns), and potentially integration with the Vision Pro spatial computing ecosystem.

The Bluetooth tracker market has become increasingly competitive. Google launched its Find My Device network for Android in 2024, enabling third-party trackers from Chipolo and other manufacturers to leverage Android's massive installed base. Samsung's SmartTag products offer similar functionality within the Galaxy ecosystem. This cross-platform competition has put downward pressure on prices across the category.

For users in the Apple ecosystem who also run their productivity on platforms with affordable Microsoft Office licence setups, AirTags represent one of many ways that Apple's hardware ecosystem extends utility beyond traditional computing.

Why This Matters

The record-low pricing matters because it makes Apple's tracking technology accessible to a broader audience at a price point that approaches impulse-purchase territory. At under AU$25 per tracker, users can practically afford to tag every frequently misplaced item — keys, wallets, bags, remote controls, even pets' collars — without significant financial commitment.

The pricing also matters as a market signal. When Apple products — which famously resist discounting — appear at record-low prices through major retailers, it typically indicates either end-of-life inventory clearing or strategic price competition. In this case, both factors are likely at play. The imminent arrival of AirTag 2 makes clearing first-generation stock a priority, while the growing competitive threat from Google's Find My Device network and Samsung's SmartTag ecosystem provides additional pricing pressure.

For the Australian market specifically, the deal is significant because Apple products tend to carry higher price premiums in Australia compared to the US market. An AU$24.75 AirTag approaches price parity with generic Bluetooth trackers that lack the Find My network's global coverage, effectively eliminating the cost argument for choosing a less capable alternative.

Industry Impact

The aggressive AirTag pricing intensifies pressure on competitors in the Bluetooth tracker space. Tile, which was acquired by Life360 in 2021, has struggled to compete against Apple's massive network advantage and must now contend with Apple matching its price points as well. Third-party tracker manufacturers building on Google's Find My Device network face a similar challenge — their primary competitive advantage has been pricing, which evaporates when Apple discounts to comparable levels.

For the broader consumer electronics market, the AirTag price drop reflects the maturation of the tracker category. What began as a premium accessory is becoming a commodity, with network effects rather than hardware innovation driving competitive advantage. The real value of an AirTag isn't the $25 piece of hardware — it's the billion-device network that makes it useful. This dynamic parallels trends across consumer technology, where hardware margins compress while ecosystem lock-in becomes the primary value driver.

Retailers are also affected. Amazon's willingness to price AirTags this aggressively suggests the company views the deal as a traffic driver that brings Apple ecosystem customers to its platform. The strategy mirrors Amazon's approach with other Apple products, where competitive pricing on popular items generates broader shopping basket revenue. Businesses running their operations on genuine Windows 11 key systems can also benefit from tracking solutions for company assets and equipment.

Companies providing enterprise productivity software and hardware solutions should note the growing trend of asset tracking technology becoming affordable enough for business inventory management applications.

Expert Perspective

Consumer electronics analysts view the AirTag pricing as confirmation that a second-generation product is imminent. Apple's retail partners typically receive guidance on inventory management ahead of new product launches, and aggressive discounting of this nature is consistent with a product transition timeline of weeks to months rather than quarters.

Privacy and security researchers note that the transition to AirTag 2 is particularly important for addressing stalking concerns. The current AirTag's speaker can be physically disabled by a determined actor, enabling covert tracking. Apple is expected to implement hardware changes in the next generation that make speaker disabling significantly more difficult, along with improved unwanted tracking notifications for both iPhone and Android users.

What This Means for Businesses

For businesses, the record-low AirTag pricing creates an opportunity to implement cost-effective asset tracking for equipment, inventory, and company vehicles. At under AU$25 per unit, the cost of tagging valuable assets is minimal compared to the potential cost of losing or misplacing them. The Find My network's global coverage means assets can be located anywhere Apple devices are present — essentially anywhere in the developed world.

However, businesses considering bulk AirTag purchases should weigh whether to buy now at discounted prices or wait for the expected second-generation model with improved features. For non-critical tracking applications, the current-generation AirTag at record-low pricing offers excellent value. For applications requiring maximum precision or enhanced security features, waiting for AirTag 2 may be prudent.

Looking Ahead

The AirTag pricing trajectory suggests the product category is approaching a tipping point where item tracking becomes as ubiquitous as smartphone cases or screen protectors. With Apple, Google, and Samsung all supporting interoperable tracking networks, the infrastructure for universal item finding is rapidly maturing. When AirTag 2 arrives — likely at the original premium price point — the current-generation product's legacy will be establishing the category at a price point that made tracking mainstream rather than aspirational.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are AirTags so cheap right now?

The record-low pricing likely reflects inventory clearing ahead of an expected second-generation AirTag launch, combined with increasing competition from Google and Samsung tracker ecosystems.

Should I buy AirTags now or wait for AirTag 2?

If you need trackers immediately and the current features meet your needs, the record-low price offers excellent value. If you want improved precision, better anti-stalking features, and the latest hardware, waiting for AirTag 2 may be worthwhile.

Do AirTags work with Android phones?

AirTags are designed for the Apple ecosystem and require an iPhone for setup and full functionality. Android users can detect nearby AirTags for anti-stalking purposes but cannot use them for personal tracking.

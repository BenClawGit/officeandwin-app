Hill and Valley Forum Reveals Silicon Valley's Surprising Embrace of Government Industrial Policy

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-28

⚡ Quick Summary

  • The 2026 Hill and Valley Forum highlighted Silicon Valley's growing acceptance of government-led industrial policy
  • Tech leaders discussed onshoring manufacturing and the strategic importance of domestic chip production
  • AI's growing unpopularity among the general public emerged as a significant concern for industry leaders
  • The forum signals a major ideological shift from tech's traditionally libertarian stance toward government partnership

What Happened

The 2026 Hill and Valley Forum has revealed a striking evolution in Silicon Valley's relationship with government. Once the epicentre of techno-libertarianism, where founders and investors viewed government intervention as an impediment to innovation, the technology industry is now actively embracing government-led industrial policy as essential to its continued growth. Attendees at this year's forum discussed themes that would have been heretical in Silicon Valley a decade ago: the necessity of government investment in manufacturing onshoring, coordinated public-private approaches to AI infrastructure, and the strategic imperative of domestic semiconductor production.

Equally noteworthy was the candid acknowledgement among tech leaders that artificial intelligence is becoming increasingly unpopular with the general public. Multiple sessions addressed the growing gap between industry enthusiasm for AI and public scepticism, with speakers recognising that the technology sector's failure to articulate AI's benefits in terms ordinary people can relate to is creating a political and regulatory risk that could constrain the industry's ambitions.

The forum's discussions reflected a technology industry that is maturing in its understanding of the political economy it operates within. The era of "move fast and break things" has given way to a more nuanced recognition that transformative technologies require societal support, regulatory frameworks, and public investment that only governments can provide at the necessary scale.

Background and Context

Silicon Valley's ideological journey from libertarian scepticism of government to active partnership represents one of the most significant shifts in the technology industry's political identity. For decades, the prevailing view among tech founders and investors was that government regulation stifled innovation and that markets, left alone, would produce optimal outcomes. This view was reinforced by the internet's explosive growth in a relatively unregulated environment and by the spectacular success of companies that operated with minimal government interaction.

The shift began in earnest with the CHIPS and Science Act of 2022, which allocated $52 billion in subsidies for domestic semiconductor manufacturing. Initially met with mixed reactions from an industry accustomed to globalised supply chains, the Act's success in attracting fabrication facilities from TSMC, Samsung, and Intel to American soil demonstrated that government industrial policy could deliver tangible results. The geopolitical tensions around Taiwan and the supply chain disruptions of the pandemic further reinforced the case for domestic manufacturing capacity.

The AI boom accelerated the realignment. Building the infrastructure required for frontier AI development — massive data centres, power generation, cooling systems, high-speed networking — requires investment at a scale and coordination that exceeds what any single company or the private sector alone can efficiently deliver. Government partnerships in permitting, power infrastructure, and workforce development have become essential enablers of AI progress, making the industry's traditional anti-government stance not just outdated but counterproductive.

Why This Matters

The convergence of Silicon Valley and government industrial policy has profound implications for how technology is developed, deployed, and regulated in the coming decade. When the industry and government are aligned on strategic priorities, the pace of investment and deployment can accelerate dramatically. The semiconductor onshoring effort, backed by both government subsidies and private capital, is producing results that neither sector could have achieved independently.

However, closer government-industry alignment also raises important questions about accountability, public benefit, and the concentration of power. If technology companies and government agencies are pursuing shared agendas, the checks and balances that traditionally operate between the private sector and the state may be weakened. The forum's discussions about AI unpopularity suggest that at least some industry leaders recognise this tension and are grappling with how to ensure that the benefits of technological progress are broadly shared. Businesses that build on widely accessible tools — a genuine Windows 11 key, standard enterprise productivity software — help democratise technology access in ways that may address some of these concerns.

Industry Impact

For the technology industry, the embrace of industrial policy creates both opportunities and obligations. Companies that align with government priorities in areas like semiconductor manufacturing, AI safety, and clean energy technology can access subsidies, favourable regulatory treatment, and government contracts. Those that resist or are seen as working against public interests risk regulatory headwinds and political backlash.

The AI unpopularity discussion is particularly consequential. If public opposition to AI strengthens, it could manifest in restrictive regulation, reduced government support, or consumer rejection of AI-powered products and services. The technology industry has experienced this dynamic before — public backlash against social media contributed to regulatory action in the EU and ongoing legislative efforts in the United States. AI companies that fail to address public concerns proactively may face similar or more severe consequences.

For enterprise technology buyers, the forum's themes reinforce the importance of investing in domestic, well-supported technology infrastructure. Products and services backed by companies with strong government relationships and domestic manufacturing presence are likely to offer greater supply chain security and regulatory stability over time.

Expert Perspective

Policy analysts who attended the forum described the ideological shift as unprecedented in the technology industry's history. The willingness of traditionally libertarian-leaning tech leaders to advocate for government intervention reflects a pragmatic recognition that the challenges and opportunities facing the industry — from AI infrastructure to semiconductor supply chains — simply cannot be addressed by the private sector alone.

The candid discussion of AI's unpopularity was also notable. Industry leaders' willingness to acknowledge the problem publicly suggests a growing awareness that the technology sector's relationship with the public is fragile and requires active management. Some speakers advocated for concrete actions — transparent AI development processes, meaningful public benefit investments, and genuine engagement with communities affected by AI deployment — rather than the public relations campaigns that have characterised past industry responses to public concern.

What This Means for Businesses

For businesses of all sizes, the evolving relationship between the technology industry and government has practical implications. Government industrial policy will increasingly shape technology availability, pricing, and supply chain reliability. Companies should monitor policy developments in areas like semiconductor manufacturing, AI regulation, and trade policy as inputs to their technology procurement strategies.

The AI unpopularity discussion also has implications for how businesses deploy AI internally and in customer-facing applications. Companies that are transparent about their AI use, that prioritise customer benefit over cost reduction, and that maintain human oversight of AI systems will be better positioned to navigate any regulatory changes or consumer sentiment shifts. Building on established, broadly trusted tools — including an affordable Microsoft Office licence with well-understood AI features — provides a stable foundation for AI adoption that minimises reputational risk.

Looking Ahead

The themes from the Hill and Valley Forum will likely intensify as the 2026 US election cycle approaches. Technology policy, AI regulation, and industrial strategy are all likely to feature prominently in political campaigns. The technology industry's newfound willingness to engage with government as a partner rather than an adversary may prove to be one of the most consequential shifts in the sector's history, shaping policy outcomes that affect every business and consumer who depends on technology.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Hill and Valley Forum?

The Hill and Valley Forum is an annual gathering that brings together technology industry leaders, policymakers, and investors to discuss the intersection of technology and government policy. The 2026 event focused on themes including industrial policy for manufacturing onshoring, AI regulation and public perception, and the evolving relationship between Silicon Valley and Washington.

Why is Silicon Valley embracing government industrial policy?

Several factors are driving this shift: the recognition that semiconductor manufacturing requires government-scale investment, national security concerns about supply chain dependence on foreign manufacturers, the CHIPS Act's success in attracting fabrication facilities to the US, and the understanding that AI infrastructure development requires coordinated public-private investment that exceeds what private markets alone can deliver.

Why is AI becoming unpopular with the public?

Growing public concern about AI stems from multiple sources: job displacement fears, privacy concerns around AI training data, the proliferation of deepfakes and misinformation, high-profile AI failures, and a general sense that the technology is being developed primarily for corporate benefit rather than public good. Tech leaders at the forum acknowledged that addressing these perceptions is critical for the industry's long-term success.

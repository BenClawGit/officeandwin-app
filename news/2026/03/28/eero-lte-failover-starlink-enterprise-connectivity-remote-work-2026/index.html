Eero Launches LTE Failover for Remote Workers as Starlink Eyes Enterprise Connectivity Market

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-28

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Amazon's Eero has launched Eero Signal, a 4G LTE backup device that provides automatic internet failover
  • The device plugs into existing Eero routers and includes 10GB annual bandwidth with Eero Plus subscriptions
  • A 5G version is in development with expanded bandwidth options up to 100GB
  • The move signals growing enterprise demand for cellular failover as remote work becomes permanent

What Happened

Amazon's mesh networking subsidiary Eero has launched Eero Signal, a compact 4G LTE device designed to provide automatic internet failover for homes and small businesses. The device plugs directly into existing Eero routers and seamlessly takes over connectivity when the primary internet service provider experiences an outage. Included with an Eero Plus subscription, users receive 10GB of annual LTE bandwidth, with upgrade options available up to 100GB for users who need more failover capacity.

The launch comes alongside the announcement that a 5G version of Eero Signal is currently in development, promising faster failover speeds and higher bandwidth capabilities when it arrives. The product represents Eero's most significant push into the business continuity space, moving beyond its traditional consumer mesh networking roots into the critical infrastructure that keeps remote workers productive during ISP outages.

Simultaneously, the broader enterprise connectivity landscape is evolving rapidly. SpaceX's Starlink satellite internet service has been quietly building its enterprise capabilities, offering a fundamentally different approach to backup connectivity that works even in locations without cellular coverage. Together, these developments signal a new era in how businesses think about internet reliability and the infrastructure that supports their distributed workforces.

Background and Context

For decades, enterprise internet failover followed a predictable playbook: contract with two different ISPs, run separate physical connections to the building, and configure the firewall to automatically switch between them. In theory, this dual-ISP approach provides redundancy. In practice, it frequently fails at the worst possible moments because both ISPs often share the same physical infrastructure — the same utility poles, the same underground conduits, the same last-mile connections.

When a backhoe cuts a fibre line or a storm takes down power to a local exchange, both ISP connections often fail simultaneously. The redundancy that enterprises paid premium prices for evaporates precisely when it is needed most. This fundamental weakness in traditional failover architecture has driven interest in alternatives that use completely independent transmission paths — cellular networks and satellite constellations that have no physical infrastructure overlap with terrestrial ISPs.

The shift to remote and hybrid work has dramatically amplified the failover challenge. When employees worked in offices, enterprises only needed to ensure connectivity at a handful of locations. Now, with workers distributed across hundreds or thousands of home offices, each individual's internet connection becomes a potential single point of failure for business operations. Ensuring reliable connectivity for a distributed workforce requires solutions that scale affordably to individual homes and small offices.

Why This Matters

Eero Signal's launch addresses a gap that has been painfully obvious since the pandemic accelerated remote work adoption. Individual remote workers have had few affordable options for automatic internet failover. Enterprise-grade solutions were prohibitively expensive for home deployment, while consumer alternatives required manual intervention to switch between connections. Eero Signal's automatic failover capability, combined with its simple plug-and-play deployment, makes reliable backup connectivity accessible at the individual worker level for the first time.

For businesses managing distributed teams, this has direct productivity implications. Every minute of internet downtime for a remote worker is a minute of lost productivity, missed meetings, and delayed deliverables. When those workers rely on cloud-based tools — enterprise productivity software like Microsoft 365, project management platforms, and video conferencing — connectivity is not a convenience but an absolute necessity. Eero Signal's approach of providing a safety net for these connections could fundamentally change how businesses think about remote work infrastructure support.

Industry Impact

The competitive dynamics in the connectivity failover space are intensifying. Eero, backed by Amazon's resources and distribution network, brings consumer-friendly pricing and ease of use. Starlink, backed by SpaceX, brings global satellite coverage that works regardless of cellular infrastructure availability. Traditional enterprise networking vendors like Cisco, Juniper, and Fortinet offer more robust but significantly more expensive solutions designed for office deployments.

The convergence of these approaches is creating a tiered market. Individual remote workers and small businesses will likely gravitate toward solutions like Eero Signal that provide adequate failover at minimal cost and complexity. Mid-market businesses may adopt a combination of cellular and satellite failover depending on their geographic distribution. Large enterprises will continue to deploy purpose-built solutions but may increasingly supplement them with consumer-grade options for their remote workforce.

For the broader technology ecosystem, reliable connectivity failover has implications beyond mere internet access. As businesses increasingly depend on cloud-based operating systems, software licences, and productivity suites, the ability to maintain connectivity becomes intertwined with the ability to do any work at all. A genuine Windows 11 key and cloud-connected productivity tools are only as useful as the internet connection that enables them.

Expert Perspective

Enterprise IT analysts view Eero Signal as an important step in democratising business continuity capabilities. Previously, automatic failover was reserved for organisations with dedicated IT staff and significant infrastructure budgets. By packaging failover into a consumer-priced accessory for an existing mesh network, Eero is making it possible for businesses of any size to provide basic connectivity resilience to their remote workers.

The 10GB annual bandwidth limitation is the primary constraint that separates Eero Signal from enterprise-grade solutions. For occasional outages lasting minutes to hours, 10GB provides ample capacity. For extended outages or heavy video conferencing usage, businesses would need the 100GB upgrade or a different solution entirely. The forthcoming 5G version may address some of these limitations with faster speeds that use less time on the cellular network for equivalent work.

What This Means for Businesses

Businesses supporting remote workers should evaluate Eero Signal as part of their connectivity resilience strategy. For teams that rely heavily on cloud-based tools including affordable Microsoft Office licences and online collaboration platforms, even brief connectivity outages can cascade into missed deadlines and disrupted workflows. Providing employees with Eero Signal as a company-funded benefit could be a cost-effective way to reduce connectivity-related productivity losses.

IT teams should also begin evaluating how cellular and satellite failover options fit into their broader disaster recovery and business continuity plans. The traditional dual-ISP approach is increasingly recognised as insufficient for modern distributed workforces, and the new generation of failover solutions offers genuinely independent redundancy paths that address the fundamental weaknesses of terrestrial-only approaches.

Key Takeaways

  • Eero Signal provides automatic 4G LTE failover for existing Eero mesh networks
  • Included with Eero Plus, it offers 10GB annual bandwidth with upgrade options to 100GB
  • A 5G version is currently in development for faster failover speeds
  • Traditional dual-ISP failover often fails because both providers share physical infrastructure
  • Remote work has created demand for affordable individual-level connectivity resilience
  • Starlink is emerging as an enterprise-grade alternative for satellite-based failover
  • The market is stratifying into consumer, mid-market, and enterprise tiers

Looking Ahead

The connectivity failover market is poised for significant growth as remote work becomes a permanent fixture of the enterprise landscape. Eero's entry into this space will likely prompt responses from other consumer networking brands, while Starlink's enterprise ambitions could disrupt the traditional failover market from the opposite direction. Businesses should expect more options, better pricing, and simpler deployment as competition intensifies throughout 2026 and beyond.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Eero Signal and how does it work?

Eero Signal is a 4G LTE backup device that plugs into existing Eero mesh routers. When your primary internet connection goes down, Eero Signal automatically switches to cellular connectivity to keep you online. It comes with 10GB of annual bandwidth included with an Eero Plus subscription, with options to upgrade to 100GB for heavier usage.

Is Eero Signal suitable for business use?

Eero Signal is currently positioned for homes and small businesses. While it provides reliable failover connectivity, larger enterprises typically require more robust solutions with higher bandwidth allowances, dedicated support, and centralized management. However, it represents an affordable entry point for small businesses and remote workers who need uninterrupted internet access.

How does Starlink compare to Eero Signal for business failover?

Starlink operates as a satellite internet service providing primary or backup connectivity, while Eero Signal is a cellular-based failover add-on for existing routers. Starlink offers higher bandwidth potential and works in areas without cellular coverage, making it better suited for enterprises with remote locations. Eero Signal is simpler to deploy and more affordable for individual remote workers and small offices.

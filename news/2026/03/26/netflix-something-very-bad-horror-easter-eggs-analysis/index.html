Netflix Horror Series Something Very Bad Is Going to Happen Hides Clever Classic Horror Easter Eggs Beyond the Obvious Blair Witch Nods

Netflix's new horror series Something Very Bad Is Going to Happen has been drawing comparisons to The Blair Witch Project since its trailer dropped, but eagle-eyed viewers are discovering that the show's real horror DNA runs much deeper. A careful analysis reveals a sophisticated web of references to classic horror cinema that suggests the creators are less interested in found-footage pastiche than in building something genuinely original from a foundation of horror history.

What Happened

Something Very Bad Is Going to Happen premiered on Netflix this week to strong viewer numbers and a polarised critical response — a combination that typically indicates a show people feel compelled to have opinions about. The series follows a couple in the days leading up to their wedding as increasingly disturbing events suggest their celebration may become something far more sinister.

The Blair Witch comparisons were immediate and inevitable. The show employs documentary-style camera work, builds dread through suggestion rather than spectacle, and features characters whose reliability as narrators becomes increasingly questionable. These surface-level similarities led many early reviews to categorise the show as a found-footage descendant, a genre that has been well-explored since Blair Witch's 1999 debut.

However, closer examination reveals a different and more interesting set of influences. TechRadar's analysis identified multiple Easter eggs pointing to a specific classic horror film — one that shares the show's interest in the horror of social rituals, the dread of committed relationships, and the way communities can become instruments of terror. Without spoiling the specific reference, the Easter eggs are embedded in set design, dialogue patterns, character names, and even the colour palette of key scenes.

The show's creators appear to be playing a deliberate game with audience expectations. By foregrounding Blair Witch references that are obvious enough for casual viewers to spot, they create a misdirection that makes the deeper references more surprising and rewarding for attentive audiences. This layered approach to horror storytelling is relatively unusual in streaming series, which often prioritise accessibility over depth.

Background and Context

Horror has become one of Netflix's most reliable genres for driving viewership and cultural conversation. The platform's horror offerings — from Stranger Things and The Haunting of Hill House to more recent successes like Wednesday and the Fear Street trilogy — consistently generate the kind of intense fan engagement and social media discussion that Netflix's algorithm-driven content strategy prizes.

The streaming era has been particularly good for horror, which thrives on the intimate viewing experience that home screens provide. Unlike action blockbusters that benefit from theatrical scale, horror's power comes from proximity and personal vulnerability — qualities that are amplified when watching alone on a laptop or phone. This has made horror content disproportionately valuable for streaming platforms relative to its production costs.

The found-footage genre, which Blair Witch Project essentially created as a commercial category, has gone through multiple cycles of popularity and exhaustion since 1999. Paranormal Activity reinvigorated the format in 2007, and subsequent entries like REC, Cloverfield, and Unfriended demonstrated the format's adaptability. But audience fatigue with the genre's conventions — shaky cameras, why-are-they-still-filming logic, night-vision climaxes — has driven creators toward hybrid approaches that use documentary aesthetics selectively rather than as a rigid framework.

Something Very Bad Is Going to Happen appears to be engaging with this evolution deliberately, using found-footage techniques as one tool among many rather than as a defining constraint. The result is a show that feels contemporary while drawing on horror traditions that predate the found-footage phenomenon by decades.

Why This Matters

The success or failure of Something Very Bad Is Going to Happen carries implications for Netflix's content strategy and the broader streaming landscape. Netflix has been investing heavily in genre content — horror, sci-fi, thriller — that generates passionate fan bases and repeat viewing. If the show succeeds in building the kind of intensive fan analysis and theory-crafting that its Easter egg structure invites, it validates a content strategy that prioritises depth and rewatch value over broad but shallow appeal.

For the horror genre specifically, the show represents an interesting test case for whether streaming audiences will embrace slow-burn, reference-heavy horror over the more immediate, jump-scare-driven content that has dominated the streaming horror landscape. The answer has implications for what kinds of horror stories get green-lit and funded across the industry.

The Easter egg culture itself has become a significant driver of engagement in the streaming era. Shows that reward close attention and repeated viewing generate disproportionate social media discussion, fan theory content, and cultural presence relative to their audience size. This dynamic favours content creators who build layered narratives with discoverable details, creating a feedback loop where attentive viewing generates online discussion that drives new viewers to the show.

Industry Impact

Netflix's continued investment in horror reinforces the genre's position as essential programming for streaming platforms. Horror content tends to be relatively inexpensive to produce — atmospheric dread is cheaper than CGI spectacle — while generating outsized audience engagement. This cost-efficiency makes horror attractive to platforms under pressure to demonstrate content spend discipline.

For content creators, the show's layered reference structure offers a template for building engagement in the attention-fragmented streaming landscape. Rather than competing for the broadest possible audience, creators can build dedicated fan communities around content that rewards deep engagement — a strategy that is often more sustainable than chasing mainstream appeal.

The technical aspects of how streaming platforms surface and recommend content like this are also relevant. Netflix's recommendation algorithm excels at connecting niche content with interested audiences, making it possible for a show like Something Very Bad Is Going to Happen to find its ideal viewers without the mass-market marketing spend that theatrical releases require.

Expert Perspective

Horror scholars and film critics have noted that the most enduring horror works are those that engage with the genre's history while adding something new. The Shining references Overlook-style architectural horror while creating something distinctly Kubrickian. Get Out draws on invasion-of-the-body-snatchers themes while delivering trenchant social commentary. Something Very Bad Is Going to Happen appears to be attempting a similar balance — acknowledging its predecessors while building something that stands on its own.

The wedding setting is particularly rich territory for horror, tapping into universal anxieties about commitment, family dynamics, and social performance. Horror that is grounded in recognisable human experiences — rather than supernatural premises — often has greater lasting impact because the source of dread is something viewers genuinely relate to rather than something they can dismiss as fantasy.

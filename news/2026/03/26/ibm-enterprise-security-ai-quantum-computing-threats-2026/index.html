IBM Issues Urgent Call for Enterprises to Prepare for Converging AI and Quantum Computing Security Threats

IBM is urging enterprises to take immediate, disciplined action to address the converging security threats posed by artificial intelligence and quantum computing, warning that organisations which wait for these technologies to mature before preparing their defences will find themselves dangerously exposed. The message, delivered at RSAC 2026 by IBM's global security leadership, frames the dual AI-quantum threat as the most significant cryptographic challenge since the advent of public-key encryption.

What Happened

Speaking at the RSA Conference 2026 in San Francisco, IBM's Mark Hughes — who leads the company's global managed security services — outlined a scenario that should concern every enterprise CISO. AI is already being weaponised by threat actors to automate vulnerability discovery, craft sophisticated phishing campaigns, and evade detection systems. Simultaneously, quantum computing is approaching a capability threshold that could render current encryption standards obsolete, potentially exposing encrypted data that has been harvested and stored by adversaries in so-called "harvest now, decrypt later" attacks.

IBM's message was notably measured: don't panic, but start moving. The company argued that the greatest risk facing enterprises is not the immaturity of these technologies but the complacency that their apparent distance from practical deployment creates. Organisations that delay cryptographic modernisation because quantum computers aren't yet powerful enough to break current encryption may find themselves unable to complete the transition before the threat materialises.

The company presented data showing that most large enterprises have thousands of cryptographic dependencies embedded across their infrastructure — from TLS certificates and VPN tunnels to database encryption, email security, and code signing. Identifying, cataloguing, and modernising all of these dependencies is a multi-year project that needs to begin now, regardless of when quantum computers achieve cryptographic relevance.

IBM also highlighted the AI security paradox: organisations must simultaneously defend against AI-powered attacks while also securing their own AI deployments. The rapid adoption of AI tools across enterprises has created new attack surfaces — from model manipulation and data poisoning to the kind of workflow hijacking recently seen in the Langflow vulnerability.

Background and Context

The cryptographic threat from quantum computing has been discussed in security circles for over a decade, but the timeline has shifted dramatically in recent years. Advances in quantum hardware from IBM, Google, and others have moved from laboratory curiosities to systems with hundreds and even thousands of qubits. While current quantum computers cannot break RSA or elliptic curve encryption, the trajectory suggests this capability could arrive within the next 5-10 years.

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has already finalised several post-quantum cryptographic standards, including ML-KEM (formerly CRYSTALS-Kyber) for key encapsulation and ML-DSA (formerly CRYSTALS-Dilithium) for digital signatures. These standards represent the replacement algorithms that organisations will need to adopt across their infrastructure — a process known as cryptographic agility or crypto-migration.

The AI threat landscape has evolved even faster. In the past 18 months, security researchers have documented AI-generated phishing emails that are virtually indistinguishable from human-written communications, AI systems that can discover and exploit software vulnerabilities faster than human security teams can patch them, and AI-powered malware that adapts its behaviour in real-time to evade detection. These capabilities are no longer theoretical — they are being actively deployed by sophisticated threat actors.

IBM's dual position as both a quantum computing developer and an enterprise security provider gives the company unique perspective on this convergence. The company's quantum computing division is actively building the machines that will eventually threaten current encryption, while its security division helps enterprises defend against exactly these threats. This creates both a credibility advantage and a commercial interest in driving urgency around cryptographic modernisation.

Why This Matters

The convergence of AI and quantum threats creates a security challenge that is qualitatively different from anything enterprises have faced before. AI accelerates the speed and sophistication of current attacks, while quantum computing threatens to undermine the mathematical foundations of encryption itself. Together, they represent a one-two punch that could overwhelm security teams that are still organised around traditional threat models.

The "harvest now, decrypt later" scenario is particularly concerning for organisations that handle sensitive data with long-term value — financial records, health information, intellectual property, government communications, and personal data. Encrypted data intercepted today could be stored by adversaries and decrypted years later when quantum computers become capable. This means the quantum threat is not a future problem — it's a present problem with a delayed impact.

For enterprise IT leaders, the message is clear: cryptographic modernisation cannot be treated as a project that starts when quantum computers become powerful enough to pose a threat. The transition to post-quantum cryptography requires years of planning, implementation, and testing across potentially thousands of systems and applications. Organisations that begin now will have the time to execute carefully; those that wait will be forced into rushed, error-prone migrations under threat conditions.

Industry Impact

IBM's public call to action at RSAC 2026 will influence enterprise security spending and strategy across the industry. CISOs who have struggled to justify investment in post-quantum cryptography preparation now have a credible, high-profile endorsement of the urgency they've been communicating to their boards.

The security vendor ecosystem is responding to the dual threat with new products and services. Companies specialising in cryptographic inventory and assessment — tools that identify where encryption is used across an enterprise and what algorithms are employed — are seeing increased demand. Post-quantum cryptography migration services are emerging as a new consulting category, and managed security providers are adding quantum-readiness assessments to their service portfolios.

Cloud providers face particular pressure, as they manage encryption for millions of enterprise customers. AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud have all announced post-quantum cryptography initiatives, but the timeline for full migration across all services remains uncertain. Enterprises using cloud services need to understand their providers' quantum-readiness timelines and supplement with their own cryptographic controls where necessary.

The insurance industry is also taking notice. Cyber insurance providers are beginning to evaluate quantum risk as a factor in coverage decisions, and organisations that can demonstrate proactive cryptographic modernisation may benefit from more favourable terms.

Expert Perspective

Cryptographers broadly agree with IBM's assessment that the time to begin post-quantum migration is now. The consensus is not that quantum computers are about to break encryption tomorrow, but that the migration process itself is so complex and time-consuming that delay creates unacceptable risk. The analogy frequently cited is the Y2K remediation effort — a massive undertaking that succeeded precisely because organisations started years before the deadline.

The AI dimension adds urgency that purely quantum-focused warnings lacked. While quantum threats operate on a years-long timeline, AI-powered attacks are happening today. The combination of immediate AI threats and approaching quantum threats creates a compelling case for comprehensive security modernisation that addresses both vectors simultaneously.

Some experts caution against IBM's commercial interest in driving quantum urgency, noting that the company sells both quantum computing services and enterprise security solutions. However, the underlying technical argument — that cryptographic migration takes years and should begin before the threat is imminent — is well-supported by independent research and endorsed by NIST, NSA, and other authoritative bodies.

What This Means for Businesses

Every business, regardless of size, should begin evaluating its cryptographic posture. For small and medium enterprises, this may be as simple as ensuring that enterprise productivity software, operating systems, and communication tools are kept up to date with the latest security patches — modern platforms like genuine Windows 11 key installations include security features designed to support cryptographic agility.

Larger organisations should initiate cryptographic inventory projects to identify all systems and applications that use encryption. This inventory becomes the foundation for a migration plan that prioritises the most sensitive data and the most exposed systems. The goal is not to complete the migration immediately but to understand the scope of the effort and begin executing against it in a disciplined, planned manner.

For businesses deploying AI tools, the security review should include assessment of AI-specific risks — from model security and data protection to workflow integrity. The productivity benefits of AI are real, but they must be balanced against the new attack surfaces that AI deployments create. Looking Ahead

The next two to three years will be critical for enterprise cryptographic modernisation. As quantum computers continue to advance and AI-powered attacks grow more sophisticated, the window for proactive preparation is narrowing. Organisations that treat this period as an opportunity — investing in cryptographic agility, AI security, and comprehensive risk assessment — will emerge in a far stronger position than those that wait for the threats to become undeniable. IBM's message at RSAC 2026 may prove to be one of the most important warnings the security industry has delivered this decade.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the harvest now decrypt later threat?

Adversaries intercept and store encrypted data today with the intention of decrypting it later when quantum computers become powerful enough to break current encryption algorithms, making it a present-day threat with future impact.

When will quantum computers be able to break current encryption?

Most experts estimate quantum computers could threaten current RSA and elliptic curve encryption within 5-10 years, though the timeline remains uncertain. IBM and others urge starting migration now because the process takes years.

What are post-quantum cryptographic standards?

NIST has finalised several post-quantum standards including ML-KEM for key encapsulation and ML-DSA for digital signatures. These algorithms are designed to resist attacks from both classical and quantum computers.

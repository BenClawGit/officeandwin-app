Apple Music Launches Dedicated Concerts Page But a Major Ticketing Flaw Dampens the Experience

Apple Music has rolled out a dedicated Concerts page that aggregates live music events, setlists, and artist touring information directly within the streaming app. While the feature represents a long-awaited addition for live music enthusiasts, a significant flaw in its ticketing integration is frustrating users who find themselves redirected through a convoluted purchasing process that undermines the seamless experience Apple typically delivers.

What Happened

The Apple Music Concerts page, which appeared in the latest app update, presents users with a curated feed of upcoming concerts based on their listening history, location, and favourite artists. The interface surfaces tour dates, venue information, and setlists from recent shows, creating a hub that connects recorded music listening with live music discovery.

The feature works elegantly in its discovery function. Users browsing an artist's page now see upcoming tour dates alongside albums and playlists. Notifications can be configured for favourite artists announcing new shows, and the Concerts page itself provides a browsable, personalised feed of live events that integrates naturally with the existing Apple Music experience.

However, the ticketing experience — arguably the most important aspect of a concerts feature — falls short. Rather than offering direct ticket purchases within the Apple Music app, the feature redirects users to external ticketing platforms through Safari. This redirect breaks the seamless flow of the Apple Music interface and introduces friction at precisely the moment when a user has decided to commit to a purchase. Multiple users have reported issues with the redirect process, including lost session data, inconsistent pricing between what's shown in Apple Music and what appears on the ticketing site, and occasional broken links for events that have sold out.

The absence of native Apple Pay integration for ticket purchases is a particularly conspicuous omission. Apple has spent years building Apple Pay into a comprehensive payment ecosystem, and a concert ticketing feature that doesn't leverage it feels like a missed opportunity — or perhaps a deliberate strategy to avoid direct competition with established ticketing platforms.

Background and Context

The intersection of music streaming and live events has been a growing focus for the industry. Spotify launched its own concerts feature in 2023, integrating with Ticketmaster to surface live events within the app. The feature has been moderately successful, helping users discover shows by artists they stream and providing Spotify with additional engagement metrics and potential revenue from ticketing partnerships.

Apple's entry into concert discovery comes later but with typically Apple ambitions for design and user experience. The company has a history of entering established categories with polished, integrated experiences that set new standards — from the iPod to the iPhone to Apple Pay itself. The Concerts page's design quality is consistent with this tradition, making its ticketing shortcomings all the more noticeable.

The live music industry has experienced a remarkable recovery since the pandemic, with concert revenue reaching record levels globally. Fans are spending more than ever on live experiences, and the average concert ticket price has increased substantially. This growth has made concert discovery and ticketing a valuable feature for any platform that can capture user attention at the moment of purchase intent.

The ticketing industry itself remains dominated by Live Nation/Ticketmaster, which controls the majority of major venue ticket sales in North America. Any company seeking to offer seamless ticket purchasing must either partner with Ticketmaster or build direct relationships with venues — both challenging propositions that may explain Apple's current redirect-based approach.

Why This Matters

Apple Music's Concerts page matters because it represents the streaming industry's continued evolution from passive music consumption to active lifestyle platform. Streaming services are no longer content to be jukeboxes; they want to be the central hub for everything music-related, from discovery and listening to merchandise, live events, and artist engagement.

The ticketing flaw is significant because it reveals the limits of even Apple's ability to deliver seamless experiences when dependent on third-party partnerships. In most of Apple's ecosystem, the company controls the end-to-end experience — hardware, software, services, and payments. Concert ticketing requires navigating a complex web of venue contracts, promoter relationships, and ticketing platform agreements that Apple cannot simply bypass with superior design.

For the broader music industry, the feature signals growing competition for the live event discovery market. As streaming platforms add concert features, traditional discovery channels — venue websites, social media announcements, word of mouth — become less important. Artists and promoters who optimise their presence on streaming platforms will gain a discovery advantage, potentially widening the gap between well-promoted acts and independent artists who rely on grassroots marketing.

Industry Impact

The launch puts competitive pressure on Spotify to enhance its own concert features. While Spotify's Ticketmaster integration offers a more direct purchasing experience, Apple Music's superior design and deeper iOS integration could attract users who value aesthetic and functional polish. The resulting competition should benefit consumers through improved features on both platforms.

For the ticketing industry, Apple Music's entry represents both opportunity and threat. A partnership with Apple could drive significant ticket sales through a premium, high-intent user base. But Apple's long-term ambitions may include native ticketing — cutting out intermediaries and capturing ticketing margins directly. The current redirect-based approach may be a transitional step rather than the permanent architecture.

Venue operators and concert promoters should pay attention to how streaming platforms surface live events. The algorithms that determine which concerts appear in a user's feed could become as important for ticket sales as traditional marketing. Understanding and optimising for these algorithms will become a necessary skill for anyone promoting live music events.

For enterprise productivity software companies and event management platforms, the trend toward integrated event discovery within consumer apps suggests growing demand for tools that help organisations manage their event presence across multiple platforms simultaneously.

Expert Perspective

Music industry analysts note that the ticketing redirect issue likely reflects ongoing negotiations between Apple and ticketing platforms rather than a technical limitation. Apple has the engineering capability to offer native ticket purchasing — Apple Pay, the App Store payment infrastructure, and Wallet integration provide all the necessary components. The current approach suggests either unresolved business terms with ticketing partners or a strategic decision to test the feature before committing to full integration.

The setlist feature, while less commercially significant, has generated positive attention from music fans. Access to recent setlists helps fans decide whether to attend upcoming shows based on what the artist is currently performing — a level of detail that traditional ticketing platforms rarely provide. This kind of contextual information demonstrates how streaming platforms can add value to the concert discovery process beyond simple event listings.

What This Means for Businesses

For businesses in the entertainment and events industry, Apple Music's Concerts page represents another channel to reach potential attendees. Ensuring accurate, up-to-date event information across all discovery platforms — including streaming services — is becoming essential for maximising ticket sales.

Companies that manage events or venues should evaluate how their events appear on Apple Music and Spotify, treating these platforms with the same attention they give to social media and search engine visibility. The audience on music streaming platforms is highly qualified — they're already listening to the artists performing — making these channels potentially more efficient than broad-based marketing.

For businesses outside the entertainment industry, the lesson is about platform integration and user experience. Apple Music's Concerts page demonstrates how features that work beautifully in isolation can stumble when they depend on external partnerships. Any business building products that integrate with third-party services should invest in making those integrations as seamless as possible — or risk the kind of user frustration that Apple is currently experiencing with its ticketing redirects. Having reliable infrastructure — from a genuine Windows 11 key to proper affordable Microsoft Office licence deployments — ensures the foundation is solid even when higher-level integrations are still evolving.

  • Feature puts competitive pressure on Spotify to enhance its own concert integration
  • Live music discovery is shifting from traditional channels to streaming platform algorithms

Looking Ahead

Apple Music's Concerts page is clearly version one of what will become a more comprehensive live music platform. The discovery and information features are strong enough to change how many users find out about upcoming shows. The ticketing experience needs significant improvement to match Apple's usual standards, and it likely will — the commercial opportunity is too large and the technical capability too readily available for Apple to leave the current friction in place indefinitely. When native ticketing arrives, it could reshape the concert discovery and purchasing journey for millions of music fans.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Apple Music Concerts page?

It's a new feature in Apple Music that aggregates upcoming concerts, tour dates, and setlists based on your listening history and location, creating a personalised hub for live music discovery.

Can you buy tickets directly in Apple Music?

Not yet. The current implementation redirects users to external ticketing platforms through Safari, which breaks the seamless Apple Music experience. Native ticketing may come in future updates.

How does Apple Music Concerts compare to Spotify's concert feature?

Apple Music offers superior design and setlist information, while Spotify has a more direct Ticketmaster integration for ticket purchasing. Both are competing to become the primary concert discovery platform for music fans.

iyO Escalates Legal Battle Against OpenAI With New Trade Secret Theft Allegations

What Happened

The legal battle between wearable AI startup iyO and OpenAI has intensified significantly with the filing of an amended lawsuit that now includes allegations of trade secret theft. The updated complaint, filed on March 26, 2026, alleges that former Apple designer Tang Tan, who co-founded OpenAI's io hardware division, was given access to iyO's confidential product designs before departing to join the rival AI company.

The original lawsuit, filed earlier this year, focused primarily on trademark confusion between iyO's brand and OpenAI's io product line. The amended complaint dramatically broadens the scope, alleging that Tang Tan participated in meetings where iyO's proprietary industrial designs, sensor configurations, and user interaction models were discussed under non-disclosure agreements — and that elements of those designs subsequently appeared in OpenAI's io device.

iyO is seeking injunctive relief that would prevent OpenAI from selling the io device in its current form, as well as substantial damages for what it characterizes as a deliberate pattern of misappropriation that began well before Tang Tan's official departure from the collaborative discussions.

Background and Context

The AI hardware market has exploded over the past two years, with multiple companies racing to create the definitive AI-powered wearable device. Following the mixed reception of Humane's AI Pin and the acquisition of Rabbit by a larger tech company, OpenAI entered the hardware market with io — a sleek wearable device designed by Tang Tan that integrates directly with OpenAI's language models to provide always-on AI assistance.

iyO had been developing its own AI wearable for over three years, with Tang Tan reportedly involved in early design consultation before he transitioned to OpenAI. The startup alleges that this consultation gave Tan access to proprietary design elements, including a unique haptic feedback system and a miniaturized sensor array that bears striking similarities to what appears in the io device.

The case touches on one of the most sensitive issues in Silicon Valley: the movement of talent between companies and the intellectual property that travels with them. While employees are generally free to use their general skills and knowledge at new employers, trade secret law draws a line at taking specific proprietary information — a line that iyO alleges was clearly crossed.

Why This Matters

This lawsuit has implications far beyond the two companies directly involved. The AI hardware market is still in its formative stage, with design standards and interaction paradigms being established by the first wave of products. If iyO's allegations are substantiated, it could chill the collaborative relationships that often exist between startups and larger companies in the pre-competitive phase of technology development.

For the broader AI industry, the case highlights the risks of the talent-driven approach to innovation that has characterized Silicon Valley for decades. When a single designer's knowledge becomes the basis for a trade secret claim, every hiring decision in the AI hardware space becomes a potential legal liability. This could slow innovation by making companies more cautious about hiring from competitors or engaging in the cross-pollination that has historically driven technological progress.

The commercial stakes are enormous. OpenAI's io device represents the company's first major hardware product and a key element of its strategy to move beyond being a pure software and API provider. A successful injunction against io would not only cost OpenAI significant revenue but could damage the company's hardware ambitions at a critical early stage. For businesses evaluating the evolving AI landscape alongside their investments in affordable Microsoft Office licence productivity tools, the outcome of this case could reshape which AI hardware platforms succeed in the enterprise.

Industry Impact

The AI hardware sector is watching this case with intense interest. If iyO prevails on the trade secret claims, it establishes a precedent that could fundamentally alter how AI hardware companies approach talent acquisition and collaborative development. Companies may need to implement more rigorous information barriers when engaging with potential design partners, and employee onboarding processes at AI hardware startups could become significantly more legally complex.

For OpenAI specifically, the timing is challenging. The company is in the process of converting from a nonprofit to a for-profit structure, and a major intellectual property lawsuit adds uncertainty to an already complicated corporate transition. Investors evaluating OpenAI's valuation will need to factor in the potential liability from this litigation, which iyO's attorneys have suggested could reach into the hundreds of millions of dollars.

The case also raises questions about the role of design in AI products. As the enterprise productivity software market increasingly incorporates AI hardware interfaces — from voice-activated assistants to wearable productivity devices — the design of these interfaces becomes a critical competitive differentiator. The iyO-OpenAI dispute suggests that design IP in the AI hardware space may be more valuable, and more fiercely contested, than many companies have assumed.

Companies investing in genuine Windows 11 key infrastructure that will eventually integrate with AI hardware accessories should monitor this case for signals about which hardware platforms will have legal freedom to operate.

Expert Perspective

Trade secret cases in hardware design are notoriously difficult to prove because they require demonstrating both that specific proprietary information was taken and that it was actually used in the competing product. iyO's amended complaint appears to be building a paper trail that connects Tang Tan's access to confidential designs with specific features in the io device — the kind of circumstantial evidence chain that courts have historically found persuasive in similar cases.

However, OpenAI will likely argue that any similarities between the products reflect independent development based on obvious design principles rather than misappropriation. The miniaturized form factor, haptic feedback, and sensor arrays that iyO claims as proprietary may well be features that any competent designer would converge on when building an AI wearable — a defense known as independent derivation that has succeeded in numerous technology trade secret cases.

What This Means for Businesses

Businesses developing AI hardware or considering partnerships with AI hardware startups should review their intellectual property protection practices in light of this case. Non-disclosure agreements should be specific about what constitutes confidential information, and companies should maintain clear documentation of their independent design processes to defend against future trade secret claims.

For businesses evaluating AI wearable devices for enterprise deployment, the uncertainty created by this lawsuit suggests a cautious approach. Committing to a hardware platform that may face an injunction carries risk, and organizations should ensure they have contingency plans for alternative devices and platforms.

Looking Ahead

The court is expected to schedule a hearing on iyO's request for a preliminary injunction within the next few weeks. If granted, OpenAI could be forced to modify or temporarily halt io device sales while the case proceeds. The discovery phase, which will involve examination of Tang Tan's communications and design files, could reveal additional details that either strengthen or undermine iyO's claims. The case is being closely watched as a potential landmark in AI hardware intellectual property law.

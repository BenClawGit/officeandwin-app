Anthropic Throttles Claude During Peak Hours as OpenAI Seizes the Moment to Drop Usage Caps

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-27

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Anthropic is throttling Claude during peak weekday hours for all tiers including $100/month Max subscribers
  • OpenAI countered by relaxing usage caps, positioning itself as the reliable alternative for business users
  • About 7% of Claude users will hit new session limits during peak business hours
  • The divergent strategies signal an inflection point in AI pricing and may push the industry toward utility-style models

AI Model Pricing Enters a New Era as Anthropic and OpenAI Take Divergent Approaches to Managing Demand

Anthropic has begun throttling usage of its Claude AI assistant during peak business hours, limiting sessions for all subscribers — including those paying $100 per month for the Max tier — during weekday periods between 5 AM and 11 AM Pacific Time. The move, announced by Anthropic technical team member Thariq Shihipar via Twitter, represents a significant shift in how AI companies manage the tension between growing demand and finite compute capacity.

In a calculated counterpunch, OpenAI has seized the moment to relax or eliminate usage caps on its competing models, positioning itself as the reliable alternative for professionals who cannot afford to lose access to AI tools during working hours. The divergent strategies offer a revealing glimpse into the economic pressures and competitive dynamics that will shape AI pricing for years to come.

According to Shihipar, the throttling adjusts five-hour session limits to deplete faster during peak periods, while weekly overall limits remain unchanged. Anthropic estimates that approximately 7% of users will hit session limits they would not have encountered before, "particularly for pro tiers." Shihipar recommended that users schedule "token-intensive background jobs" for off-peak hours — a suggestion that underscores just how constrained Anthropic's compute capacity has become.

Background and Context

The peak-hour throttling represents the latest chapter in Anthropic's ongoing struggle to balance surging demand with available infrastructure. The company had previously attempted a carrot approach, offering double the standard rate limits during off-peak hours to incentivise users to shift their usage. The move to peak-hour throttling signals that positive incentives were insufficient and Anthropic has resorted to what Shihipar characterised as necessary demand management.

The timing is particularly notable because it comes during a period of explosive growth in AI adoption. Claude has become a critical tool for software developers, researchers, analysts, and writers who rely on it throughout the workday. Throttling these users during the exact hours they need the tool most creates a significant friction that competitors are eager to exploit.

OpenAI's counter-move is strategically shrewd. By relaxing caps precisely when Anthropic is tightening them, OpenAI positions ChatGPT and its API as the dependable workhorse that will not abandon users mid-task. For professionals evaluating which AI platform to commit to, reliability during business hours may prove more important than marginal differences in model capability.

Why This Matters

This moment marks a critical inflection point in the AI industry's maturation. For the first time, a leading AI company is explicitly rationing access to its product — not based on willingness to pay, but based on when users want to use it. Even $100/month Max subscribers face the same restrictions, suggesting that Anthropic's compute constraints are genuinely binding and cannot be resolved through pricing alone.

For businesses that have integrated AI tools into their workflows, this development is a wake-up call about platform dependency risk. An organisation that has built processes around Claude's capabilities now faces the prospect of degraded access during the most productive hours of the workday. This is analogous to a cloud provider throttling compute during business hours — it fundamentally undermines the value proposition of the service.

The broader implications for AI pricing are significant. As models become more capable and more expensive to run, the current subscription model — where users pay a flat monthly fee for functionally unlimited access — may prove unsustainable. The industry may be moving toward a future where AI access is priced more like electricity: with peak and off-peak rates, capacity reservations, and usage-based billing that reflects the true cost of compute. Businesses relying on enterprise productivity software with integrated AI features should factor these evolving pricing dynamics into their technology planning.

Industry Impact

The competitive dynamics unleashed by Anthropic's throttling decision could reshape the AI market. Google's Gemini, Meta's Llama-based offerings, and a growing ecosystem of open-source models all stand to benefit as users diversify their AI tool portfolios to avoid single-platform dependency. The era of choosing one AI assistant and committing to it exclusively may be ending.

For enterprise customers, the implications are particularly acute. Companies that have standardised on Claude for development, analysis, or content creation face a dilemma: accept degraded peak-hour performance, switch to a competitor, or develop multi-platform strategies that route work to whichever provider has available capacity. This kind of load-balancing across AI providers is a new challenge that enterprise IT teams had not anticipated needing to solve.

The API market will also feel reverberations. Developers building products on Claude's API may need to implement fallback mechanisms that switch to alternative models during peak periods, adding complexity and cost to AI-powered applications. Businesses building on AI platforms — much like those managing affordable Microsoft Office licence deployments — must now consider provider reliability as a critical factor alongside model capability and pricing.

Expert Perspective

AI industry analysts have framed Anthropic's decision as evidence that the current economics of large language models are not sustainable at current pricing levels. Training and serving frontier AI models requires enormous compute infrastructure, and the subscription fees — even at $100/month — may not cover the marginal cost of serving heavy users during peak demand.

The comparison to early cloud computing is instructive. AWS and Azure went through similar growing pains in their early years, eventually solving capacity constraints through massive infrastructure investment and sophisticated demand management. Anthropic may be in an analogous phase, and peak-hour throttling could be a temporary measure until the company's infrastructure catches up with demand. However, in a competitive market, temporary inconveniences can permanently shift user allegiances.

What This Means for Businesses

Organisations that rely on AI tools for mission-critical workflows should immediately assess their platform dependency and develop contingency plans. This means maintaining accounts with multiple AI providers, testing workflow compatibility across platforms, and identifying which tasks can be shifted to off-peak hours without significant productivity impact.

For individual professionals, the message is clear: diversify your AI toolkit. Maintaining proficiency with multiple AI assistants — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and others — provides resilience against any single platform's capacity constraints. Similarly, ensuring your genuine Windows 11 key workstation environment is set up with multiple AI integrations gives you flexibility to switch between services as availability dictates.

Looking Ahead

The tension between AI demand and compute supply is unlikely to resolve quickly. While Anthropic and other AI companies are investing heavily in infrastructure, the pace of demand growth has consistently outstripped capacity expansion. Expect more AI providers to experiment with demand management techniques — including dynamic pricing, capacity reservations, and priority queues — as the industry searches for sustainable business models that balance access, quality, and profitability.

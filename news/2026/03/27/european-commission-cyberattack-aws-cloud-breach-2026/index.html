European Commission Confirms Major Cyberattack After Hackers Breach AWS Cloud Infrastructure

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-27

⚡ Quick Summary

  • European Commission confirmed a cyberattack on its AWS cloud infrastructure with hackers claiming 350+ GB of stolen data
  • This is the second major breach in two months, following a January incident exploiting Ivanti vulnerabilities
  • Attackers plan to leak data publicly rather than extort, suggesting political or ideological motivation
  • AWS stated its own infrastructure was not compromised, pointing to the Commission's cloud configuration as the vector

EU Executive Body Acknowledges Cloud Infrastructure Breach as Hackers Claim Hundreds of Gigabytes Stolen

The European Commission has confirmed that it suffered a significant cyberattack targeting its Amazon Web Services cloud infrastructure, with hackers claiming to have exfiltrated more than 350 gigabytes of data including multiple databases. The breach, disclosed on Friday March 27, 2026, represents the second major security incident to hit the EU's top executive body in less than two months.

European Commission spokesperson Nika Blazevic confirmed to TechCrunch that the Commission "discovered a cyber-attack, which affected part of our cloud infrastructure," adding that "immediate steps" were taken to contain the attack and implement risk mitigation measures. In a longer statement published on the Commission's website, officials clarified that the breach "affected its cloud infrastructure hosting the Commission's web presence on the Europa.eu platform."

The threat actor responsible for the attack contacted Bleeping Computer earlier in the week, providing screenshots as proof of access to European Commission employee information and an internal email server. Notably, the attacker stated they have no intention of extorting the Commission but plan to leak the stolen data publicly at a later date — a tactic that suggests the motivation may be political or ideological rather than financial.

Background and Context

This breach comes on the heels of another data breach disclosed by the European Commission in February 2026, when officials discovered that the mobile device management platform used to manage staff devices had been compromised. That January incident has been linked to attacks targeting other European institutions, including the Dutch Data Protection Authority and Valtori, a Finnish government agency, all exploiting vulnerabilities in Ivanti Endpoint Manager Mobile software.

The pattern of repeated attacks against European institutional infrastructure reflects a broader trend of state-sponsored and ideologically motivated hacking groups targeting government entities. The timing, coinciding with escalating geopolitical tensions related to the Iran conflict and ongoing Russia-Ukraine dynamics, raises questions about whether these attacks are coordinated elements of a larger cyber campaign against Western institutions.

AWS, for its part, has distanced itself from the incident. "AWS did not experience a security event, and our services operated as designed," an AWS spokesperson told Bleeping Computer. This suggests the breach exploited the Commission's own cloud configuration, credentials, or access management practices rather than a vulnerability in AWS itself — a distinction that underscores the shared responsibility model of cloud security.

Why This Matters

The breach of a major governmental body's cloud infrastructure sends a stark message to organisations of all sizes: cloud migration does not eliminate security responsibilities — it transforms them. The European Commission presumably has access to world-class cybersecurity resources and expertise, yet still fell victim to a breach that exposed hundreds of gigabytes of sensitive data. If the EU's executive body can be compromised, any organisation is potentially vulnerable.

For businesses operating in the European market, this incident has regulatory implications. The European Commission is the body responsible for enforcing the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and proposing new digital legislation. A breach of the Commission's own infrastructure undermines its authority on data protection and could influence the tone and direction of upcoming cybersecurity regulations. Organisations that handle European data should be preparing for potentially stricter requirements in the wake of this embarrassment.

The incident also highlights the critical importance of proper cloud security configuration. Businesses managing their IT infrastructure — from genuine Windows 11 key deployments to cloud-hosted services — must ensure that access controls, encryption, and monitoring are properly implemented. The shared responsibility model means that cloud providers secure the infrastructure, but customers are responsible for securing their data and access within it.

Industry Impact

The cybersecurity industry is likely to see increased demand for cloud security posture management (CSPM) tools and services following this high-profile breach. When a breach of this magnitude hits an institution as prominent as the European Commission, it catalyses procurement decisions across the public and private sectors. CISOs who have been advocating for increased cloud security budgets now have a powerful case study to present to their boards.

The cloud computing industry itself faces a trust challenge. While AWS correctly notes that its infrastructure was not compromised, the headline-level narrative — "European Commission's AWS account hacked" — inevitably creates negative associations. Cloud providers will need to invest more heavily in customer education, default security configurations, and proactive monitoring to prevent their platforms from becoming the vector through which high-profile breaches are executed.

For enterprise productivity software vendors and service providers, this breach reinforces the value proposition of security-hardened configurations, multi-factor authentication, and zero-trust architecture. The era of assuming that cloud infrastructure is inherently secure is definitively over.

Expert Perspective

Cybersecurity professionals have noted that the attacker's decision to leak data rather than pursue extortion suggests a sophisticated operator with objectives beyond financial gain. This pattern is consistent with hacktivism or state-sponsored operations where the goal is to embarrass, destabilise, or exert political pressure rather than to monetise stolen data.

The rapid succession of breaches — the Ivanti-related incident in January followed by the AWS breach in March — suggests systemic security weaknesses in the Commission's infrastructure management practices. Security experts recommend that organisations experiencing serial breaches conduct a comprehensive security architecture review rather than treating each incident as isolated, as the root causes often trace back to shared vulnerabilities in identity management, network segmentation, or security monitoring.

What This Means for Businesses

Every organisation using cloud infrastructure should treat this incident as a wake-up call. The specific lessons are clear: audit cloud access controls regularly, implement least-privilege access policies, enable comprehensive logging and monitoring, and ensure that sensitive data is encrypted both at rest and in transit. These are not advanced security practices — they are baseline requirements that, when neglected, leave even the most prominent institutions vulnerable.

For businesses using Microsoft's ecosystem, including affordable Microsoft Office licence deployments with cloud-connected features, ensuring that Conditional Access policies, MFA, and data loss prevention rules are properly configured is essential. The European Commission's breach demonstrates that the gap between having cloud infrastructure and having secure cloud infrastructure can be measured in hundreds of gigabytes of stolen data.

Looking Ahead

The European Commission's investigation is ongoing, and the full scope of the data breach remains unclear. The attacker's stated intention to leak the data publicly means that the fallout from this incident will continue to unfold in the weeks and months ahead. For the Commission, restoring credibility on cybersecurity will require not just fixing the immediate vulnerabilities but demonstrating a comprehensive overhaul of its security posture. For the rest of the industry, this breach serves as a sobering reminder that no organisation is too large or too important to be compromised.

Frequently Asked Questions

What data was stolen in the European Commission breach?

Hackers claim to have stolen over 350 gigabytes of data including multiple databases, employee information, and access to an internal email server used by Commission employees.

Was AWS itself hacked?

No. AWS stated that it did not experience a security event and its services operated as designed. The breach exploited the European Commission's own cloud configuration and access management.

Is this related to previous European Commission breaches?

The Commission disclosed a separate breach in February stemming from a January hack of its mobile device management platform, linked to Ivanti software vulnerabilities that also affected other European institutions.

Samsung Signals Major AI Strategy Shift With Plans for Broader AI Partnerships

Samsung Signals Major AI Strategy Shift With Plans for Broader AI Partnerships

What Happened

Samsung's consumer device chief TM Roh has publicly declared that the South Korean electronics giant is "open to strategic co-operation" with additional artificial intelligence companies, marking a significant departure from the company's historically cautious approach to third-party AI integration. The announcement comes on the heels of Samsung's recent decision to integrate Perplexity, the AI-powered search engine, directly into its mobile operating system.

The move represents a strategic pivot for Samsung, which has traditionally relied on its own Bixby assistant and selective partnerships with Google for AI capabilities on its Galaxy devices. By embedding Perplexity into its mobile OS, Samsung has demonstrated a willingness to look beyond its existing partnerships to deliver cutting-edge AI experiences to its estimated 1.1 billion active device users worldwide.

Roh's comments, made during an interview with the Financial Times, suggest that Samsung views the current AI landscape as too dynamic and fast-moving for any single company to dominate alone. The executive indicated that Samsung is actively evaluating multiple AI providers across various domains, including natural language processing, computer vision, and generative AI capabilities.

Background and Context

Samsung's AI journey has been marked by both ambition and frustration. The company launched Bixby in 2017, initially positioning it as a direct competitor to Apple's Siri and Google Assistant. However, Bixby struggled to gain traction with consumers, who found its capabilities limited compared to rival assistants. Despite significant investment, Bixby never achieved the market penetration Samsung had hoped for.

The broader smartphone industry has undergone a dramatic transformation since the emergence of large language models in late 2022. Apple integrated its Apple Intelligence suite across its ecosystem, Google doubled down on Gemini across Android, and Chinese manufacturers like Xiaomi and Oppo have forged partnerships with domestic AI providers. Samsung found itself at a crossroads: continue investing solely in proprietary AI or embrace an open ecosystem approach.

The Perplexity integration is particularly noteworthy because it represents a direct challenge to Google's search dominance on Android devices. While Samsung's Galaxy phones run on Android, the company has long sought to differentiate its software experience. Adding Perplexity as a native AI search option gives Samsung users an alternative to Google's search engine, potentially reshaping how hundreds of millions of people discover information.

Why This Matters

Samsung's openness to multiple AI partnerships could fundamentally reshape the competitive dynamics of the smartphone industry. For years, the mobile AI landscape has been defined by vertical integration, with Apple controlling its entire AI stack and Google leveraging its search and cloud infrastructure. Samsung's multi-partner approach introduces a new model: the AI aggregator.

This strategy carries significant implications for businesses that depend on the mobile ecosystem. If Samsung successfully integrates multiple best-in-class AI providers into its devices, it could create a more competitive marketplace for AI services, driving down costs and accelerating innovation. Companies that rely on enterprise productivity software should pay close attention, as Samsung's approach could influence how AI features are delivered across all business tools, from email clients to document editors.

The move also signals a broader industry trend toward AI interoperability. Rather than locking users into a single AI ecosystem, Samsung appears to be betting that consumers and businesses want choice. This aligns with growing enterprise demand for AI solutions that work across platforms and providers, reducing vendor lock-in and increasing flexibility.

Industry Impact

The ripple effects of Samsung's announcement extend well beyond the smartphone market. AI companies that previously had limited distribution channels now have a potential pathway to reach Samsung's massive user base. For startups and mid-size AI firms, a Samsung partnership could provide the scale needed to compete with tech giants like Google, Microsoft, and OpenAI.

For Google specifically, Samsung's strategy presents a complex challenge. Google pays Samsung billions annually to maintain its default search position on Galaxy devices. If Samsung begins routing more queries through alternative AI providers like Perplexity, Google's search advertising revenue could face pressure. This dynamic adds another dimension to the already intense regulatory scrutiny of Google's search distribution agreements.

Hardware manufacturers across the industry are likely watching Samsung's moves closely. If the multi-AI approach proves successful in driving device sales and user engagement, competitors from Xiaomi to Motorola may adopt similar strategies. This could accelerate the fragmentation of the AI assistant market, creating both opportunities and challenges for developers building AI-powered applications.

The enterprise market stands to benefit significantly from this trend. Businesses using Samsung devices, including those running affordable Microsoft Office licence solutions alongside Samsung's mobile ecosystem, could see improved AI capabilities across their workflow without being locked into a single provider.

Expert Perspective

Industry analysts view Samsung's move as both strategically sound and operationally complex. Managing multiple AI partnerships requires careful integration work, consistent user experience design, and clear data governance policies. Samsung will need to ensure that user data is handled appropriately across all AI partners, a challenge that becomes more complex with each additional integration.

The timing of Samsung's announcement is also significant. With Apple Intelligence maturing and Google's Gemini becoming deeply embedded in Android, Samsung needed a differentiated AI strategy. By positioning itself as an open platform for AI innovation, Samsung creates a compelling narrative for both consumers and enterprise buyers who value flexibility over ecosystem lock-in.

Security experts note that multi-AI device integration raises important questions about data privacy and protection. Businesses deploying Samsung devices with genuine Windows 11 key installations alongside their mobile fleet will want assurances that sensitive data processed by multiple AI providers remains secure and compliant.

What This Means for Businesses

For enterprise technology decision-makers, Samsung's AI partnership strategy creates immediate planning considerations. Organizations with large Samsung device deployments should evaluate how new AI integrations might affect their mobile device management policies, data security frameworks, and acceptable use guidelines.

Small and medium businesses stand to benefit most from Samsung's approach. Rather than paying for premium AI services individually, SMBs could access multiple AI capabilities through their Samsung devices at no additional cost. This democratization of AI tools could help level the playing field between large enterprises and smaller competitors.

Key Takeaways

  • Samsung's TM Roh confirms the company is actively seeking additional AI partnerships beyond its existing Google and Perplexity integrations
  • The strategy represents a shift from proprietary AI development to an open-platform aggregator model
  • Google's search distribution revenue on Samsung devices could face pressure as alternative AI providers gain integration
  • Enterprise buyers may benefit from increased AI choice and reduced vendor lock-in on Samsung devices
  • The multi-AI approach could set a new standard for how smartphone manufacturers deliver AI features
  • Data privacy and security governance become more complex with multiple AI provider integrations

Looking Ahead

Samsung's expanded AI partnership strategy is still in its early stages, but the trajectory is clear. Expect to see additional AI provider announcements throughout 2026, likely spanning specialized domains such as health AI, creative tools, and enterprise productivity. The key question will be whether Samsung can deliver a seamless, unified AI experience across multiple providers, or whether the complexity of integration creates a fragmented user experience. For now, the industry is watching closely as Samsung charts a new course in the AI era.

Frequently Asked Questions

What AI companies has Samsung partnered with so far?

Samsung has integrated Google's AI services through Android and recently added Perplexity AI search directly into its mobile operating system, with plans to expand to additional AI providers.

How does Samsung's AI strategy differ from Apple's approach?

While Apple pursues vertical integration with its proprietary Apple Intelligence suite, Samsung is adopting an open-platform model that aggregates multiple third-party AI providers to offer users greater choice and flexibility.

Will Samsung's new AI partnerships affect business users?

Yes, enterprise users with Samsung device deployments may benefit from access to multiple AI capabilities, though IT administrators should review data governance and security policies as new AI integrations are added.

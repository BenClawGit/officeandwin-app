New MacBook Air M5 Arrives With Double Storage and Higher Starting Prices Across Every Model

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-03

⚡ Quick Summary

  • MacBook Air M5 doubles base storage to 512GB while raising starting prices by $100
  • Display brightness upgraded to 600 nits across both 13-inch and 15-inch models
  • M5 chip brings improved Neural Engine for AI tasks with 18-hour battery life maintained
  • Price gap below $1,199 signals potential sub-$1,000 budget MacBook in Apple's pipeline

What Happened

Apple has refreshed its entire MacBook Air lineup with the M5 chip, doubling base storage across all configurations while simultaneously increasing starting prices. The 13-inch MacBook Air now begins at $1,199 with 16GB of unified memory and 512GB of storage, up from the previous $1,099 entry point with 256GB. The 15-inch model starts at $1,399, also with doubled storage but a $100 price increase.

The updated machines retain the same external design language introduced with the M2 MacBook Air in 2022, including the slim wedge-free chassis, MagSafe charging, and the notched display. However, Apple has upgraded the display brightness to 600 nits across both sizes, a meaningful improvement for outdoor and bright-environment usage that addresses one of the few consistent criticisms of the previous generation.

The M5 chip inside brings the same efficiency improvements seen across the broader M5 family, including improved single-threaded performance, a faster Neural Engine for on-device AI tasks, and enhanced media engines capable of hardware-accelerated AV1 encoding. Apple states the new Air delivers up to 18 hours of battery life, matching the M4 generation despite the performance improvements.

Background and Context

The MacBook Air has evolved from Apple's ultraportable compromise machine into its best-selling computer, accounting for an estimated 60 percent of all Mac sales. The product's success has been built on the formula of sufficient performance for the vast majority of users packaged in the thinnest and lightest form factor Apple offers.

The storage doubling addresses a long-standing criticism: Apple's base storage tiers have been notoriously conservative, with the 256GB entry point increasingly inadequate as application sizes grow, particularly with AI models and features that require significant local storage. The price increases, however, create an interesting strategic gap at the lower end of the Mac lineup, fueling speculation about a rumored sub-$1,000 MacBook that could target education and price-sensitive consumer markets.

For users who rely on enterprise productivity software for work, the doubled storage is particularly relevant — modern productivity suites, cloud sync folders, and AI-powered features all demand significantly more local storage than they did even two years ago.

Why This Matters

The pricing strategy reveals Apple's confidence in the Mac's value proposition at premium price points while simultaneously clearing room for a potential budget offering. By making 512GB the new baseline, Apple eliminates the most common point of buyer's remorse and reduces the number of customers who discover within months that their storage is inadequate.

The move also reflects the reality of computing in 2026, where on-device AI models, high-resolution photo libraries, and increasingly large application bundles mean that 256GB of storage constrains the user experience in ways that weren't true even three years ago. For productivity-focused users managing documents, spreadsheets, and presentations through tools like Microsoft Office, the additional headroom ensures smooth operation without constant storage management. Pairing the new MacBook Air with an affordable Microsoft Office licence creates a capable and complete productivity workstation at a competitive total cost of ownership.

Industry Impact

Apple's price increases put pressure on the premium Windows ultrabook market, where competitors like the Dell XPS 13, Lenovo Yoga Slim 7, and Microsoft Surface Laptop have been carefully positioning against the MacBook Air's previous price points. Those manufacturers now face a choice: match Apple's storage generosity (which increases their bill of materials) or maintain current pricing and risk appearing inferior in specification comparisons.

The storage doubling also accelerates the broader industry trend toward higher base storage tiers. Samsung and SK Hynix, the primary NAND flash suppliers, have been ramping 200-plus layer 3D NAND production throughout 2025, and Apple's volume commitment at 512GB minimum helps drive economies of scale that benefit the entire market. For the enterprise PC market, this shift validates what IT procurement teams have known for years: base storage configurations have been inadequate, and the industry is finally catching up to real-world requirements.

Expert Perspective

The strategic gap left below $1,199 is arguably the most interesting development. Apple hasn't had a sub-$1,000 laptop since discontinuing the original MacBook in 2019, and the education market — where Chromebooks and budget Windows laptops dominate — represents a significant growth opportunity. By pushing the Air upmarket with better specifications, Apple creates manufacturing and pricing room for a more accessible entry point without cannibalizing the Air's margins.

The display brightness improvement to 600 nits, while seemingly minor, signals Apple's attention to the growing number of remote and hybrid workers who need reliable visibility in varied lighting conditions — a practical consideration that specification sheets often overlook but users deeply appreciate in daily use.

What This Means for Businesses

For enterprise procurement, the new pricing structure simplifies purchasing decisions. The elimination of the 256GB tier means fewer configuration mistakes and reduced support tickets from users running out of storage. IT departments managing mixed Mac and Windows fleets will appreciate that the 512GB base provides adequate room for productivity suites, virtualization through Parallels, and the growing storage demands of AI-powered features.

Organizations considering fleet refreshes should evaluate the total cost of ownership, including a genuine Windows 11 key for virtualization needs, against the MacBook Air's improved specifications. The 18-hour battery life and fanless design remain compelling for mobile workforces, and the M5's Neural Engine improvements position the Air as capable of handling the AI-enhanced productivity features that Microsoft and other vendors are rapidly integrating into their software.

Key Takeaways

  • MacBook Air M5 doubles base storage to 512GB across all models
  • Starting prices increase by $100, with 13-inch at $1,199 and 15-inch at $1,399
  • Display brightness improved to 600 nits for better outdoor visibility
  • M5 chip delivers improved AI performance via upgraded Neural Engine
  • Price gap below $1,199 fuels speculation about a budget MacBook
  • 18-hour battery life maintained despite performance improvements

Looking Ahead

The MacBook Air M5 positions Apple's mainstream laptop as a more complete package, but the real story may be what comes next. If Apple introduces a sub-$1,000 MacBook later in 2026, the Air's upmarket move will look like careful strategic groundwork. For now, buyers upgrading from M1 or M2 era machines will find the M5 Air a meaningful improvement, while those on M3 or M4 models may want to wait for the anticipated MacBook Air redesign expected in 2027.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does the new MacBook Air M5 cost?

The 13-inch MacBook Air M5 starts at $1,199 and the 15-inch model starts at $1,399, representing a $100 increase over the previous generation, but now includes 512GB of base storage instead of 256GB.

What are the main improvements in the MacBook Air M5?

Key improvements include the M5 chip with enhanced Neural Engine, doubled base storage to 512GB, increased display brightness to 600 nits, and maintained 18-hour battery life despite the performance gains.

Is the MacBook Air M5 worth upgrading to from the M4 model?

For M4 MacBook Air owners, the M5 upgrade is incremental. The most compelling upgrades are for users on M1 or M2 era machines who will benefit from significantly improved performance, storage, and display brightness.

Beat Saber Joins Meta Horizon Plus VR Subscription as Meta Consolidates Its Quest Gaming Strategy

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-03

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Beat Saber joins Meta Horizon Plus VR subscription at $14.99/month
  • First time the iconic VR game is available outside direct purchase
  • Music packs rotate quarterly to maintain ongoing subscription engagement
  • Move signals Meta's push to establish subscriptions as VR's primary content model

What Happened

Meta has announced that Beat Saber, widely regarded as virtual reality's most iconic and commercially successful game, will be added to the Meta Horizon Plus subscription service. The inclusion gives subscribers access to the full game and its music packs as part of the monthly subscription fee, marking the first time Beat Saber has been available outside of direct purchase since its launch in 2018.

Meta Horizon Plus, which costs $14.99 per month, provides access to a rotating library of VR games and experiences alongside exclusive content and early access to new titles. Adding Beat Saber — which has sold millions of copies at $29.99 — represents the service's most significant title acquisition and signals Meta's intention to make the subscription the primary way users engage with VR content on Quest headsets.

The move comes as Meta continues to invest heavily in its VR ecosystem despite the broader mixed reality market's slower-than-expected growth. Existing Beat Saber owners retain their purchased copies and all DLC, while new subscribers gain access to the base game and a selection of music packs that rotates quarterly.

Background and Context

Beat Saber has been VR's killer app since its early access launch in 2018. The rhythm game, which tasks players with slicing through colored blocks with light sabers to the beat of music, became the first VR-exclusive title to generate over $100 million in revenue. Meta acquired Beat Saber's developer, Beat Games, in 2019, making the title a first-party exclusive for the Quest platform.

Meta Horizon Plus launched in 2025 as the company's answer to Xbox Game Pass and PlayStation Plus — a subscription model designed to reduce the friction of trying VR games and increase user engagement with Quest headsets. The service has struggled to attract subscribers, with industry estimates placing the subscriber count well below Meta's internal targets, partly because the VR game library lacks the depth and breadth of console ecosystems.

For VR users who also rely on enterprise productivity software in their professional lives, the growing maturity of VR subscription models reflects a broader trend toward consumption-based access across all technology categories.

Why This Matters

Adding Beat Saber to Horizon Plus is Meta's strongest play yet to drive subscription adoption. The game serves as VR's primary gateway title — the experience that converts VR-curious consumers into regular headset users — and placing it behind a subscription rather than a one-time purchase fundamentally changes the economics of VR onboarding. Instead of paying $30 upfront for a game they might not love, new Quest owners can try Beat Saber as part of a $15 subscription that also includes dozens of other titles.

This strategy mirrors what Xbox Game Pass achieved for console gaming: by lowering the cost of trying individual games, subscriptions increase overall engagement, which in turn drives spending on DLC, in-app purchases, and hardware upgrades. If Beat Saber's subscription availability meaningfully increases Horizon Plus adoption, Meta gains a recurring revenue stream that is far more predictable and valuable than one-time game sales.

Industry Impact

The VR gaming industry is watching this move carefully as a test case for subscription-based VR content. If Beat Saber drives significant subscriber growth, other VR developers may feel pressured to license their titles to Horizon Plus, accelerating the shift toward subscription-based VR gaming and potentially reducing the viability of premium-priced standalone VR game launches.

For VR hardware competitors like Sony (PlayStation VR2) and Apple (Vision Pro), Meta's subscription strategy raises competitive stakes. A compelling subscription library makes Quest headsets more attractive relative to platforms that rely on traditional game sales, particularly for price-sensitive consumers who view a monthly subscription as less risky than building a VR game library at $30-$60 per title. An affordable Microsoft Office licence follows a similar value logic — giving users access to premium software at a fraction of what individual application purchases would cost.

Expert Perspective

VR industry analysts see the Beat Saber inclusion as a defensive move as much as an offensive one. Quest headset sales have plateaued in recent quarters, and Meta needs to demonstrate growing engagement metrics to justify its continued multi-billion-dollar annual investment in Reality Labs. Beat Saber's proven ability to drive headset usage — the average Beat Saber player uses their Quest headset significantly more often than non-players — makes it the highest-leverage addition to Horizon Plus's catalog.

The decision to include music packs on a rotating basis rather than providing permanent access to all DLC is also strategically notable. It creates an incentive for subscribers to maintain their subscription month over month to catch different music packs, while preserving the direct purchase option for players who want permanent access to specific packs.

What This Means for Businesses

Enterprise VR adoption, while still niche, has been growing in training and collaboration applications. Meta's investment in its subscription ecosystem signals continued commitment to the Quest platform, which reassures businesses evaluating VR for training simulations, virtual meetings, and design visualization. A healthy consumer VR ecosystem supports the hardware volumes and developer investment that make enterprise VR applications economically viable.

Companies exploring VR for team building or wellness programs may find the Horizon Plus subscription an efficient way to provide employees with a range of VR experiences, including Beat Saber's physical workout benefits, without managing individual game licenses. Combined with genuine Windows 11 key deployments for VR development workstations, businesses can build comprehensive VR workflows at manageable costs.

Key Takeaways

  • Beat Saber added to Meta Horizon Plus subscription at $14.99 per month
  • First time Beat Saber available outside direct purchase since 2018 launch
  • Move designed to drive Horizon Plus subscriber growth and Quest engagement
  • Music packs rotate quarterly, creating ongoing subscription incentive
  • Existing Beat Saber owners retain their purchased copies and all DLC
  • Test case for subscription-based VR content distribution

Looking Ahead

Beat Saber's addition to Horizon Plus will be closely monitored as a bellwether for VR subscription models. If subscriber growth meets Meta's targets, expect more first-party and high-profile third-party titles to follow, potentially establishing subscriptions as the dominant business model for VR content within the next two years. For the VR industry, the outcome will influence investment decisions, development priorities, and pricing strategies across the ecosystem.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Beat Saber now free with Meta Horizon Plus?

Beat Saber is included as part of the Meta Horizon Plus subscription at $14.99 per month. Subscribers get access to the base game and rotating music packs. Existing owners retain their purchased copies and all DLC.

How much does Meta Horizon Plus cost?

Meta Horizon Plus costs $14.99 per month and includes access to a library of VR games and experiences, exclusive content, and early access to new titles, now including Beat Saber.

Do existing Beat Saber owners lose anything?

No. Existing Beat Saber owners retain their purchased copies and all downloaded DLC. The subscription addition is an alternative access method for new users, not a replacement for direct ownership.

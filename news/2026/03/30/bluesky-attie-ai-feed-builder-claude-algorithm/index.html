Bluesky Launches Attie: The AI Feed Builder Powered by Claude That Could Reshape Social Algorithms

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-30

⚡ Quick Summary

  • Bluesky unveiled Attie, an AI assistant that builds custom social media feed algorithms from natural language descriptions.
  • Attie is powered by Anthropic's Claude and runs on Bluesky's open AT Protocol infrastructure.
  • The product democratises feed creation, removing the coding barrier from Bluesky's Feed Generator ecosystem.
  • Attie sets a new standard for algorithmic transparency that puts competitive pressure on major social platforms.

Bluesky has unveiled Attie, a new AI assistant that lets users build customised social media feeds through natural language conversation. Built on Anthropic's Claude and running on the AT Protocol, Attie represents a fundamentally different approach to algorithmic curation — one that puts users in the driver's seat in a way no major social platform has attempted before.

What Happened

At the Atmosphere conference in late March 2026, Bluesky's former CEO Jay Graber — now serving as chief innovation officer — and CTO Paul Frazee unveiled Attie, a standalone AI assistant application built by Bluesky's newly formed Exploration team. Attie allows users to describe the kind of content they want to see in natural language: "Show me tech news but not AI hype" or "Curate my feed for deep reads, nothing under 5 minutes" — and the AI translates those descriptions into functional feed algorithms running on the AT Protocol.

The product is notable on multiple levels. Technically, it demonstrates the maturity of the AT Protocol as a platform for third-party innovation — Attie works with any Bluesky account and can create feeds that other users can subscribe to, not just the account owner. Strategically, it signals that Bluesky is moving beyond being a Twitter alternative and positioning itself as a platform for building novel social experiences on open infrastructure.

Powered by Anthropic's Claude, Attie maintains a conversational interface for refining feeds over time. Users can tell Attie that a feed is "too much news, not enough personal posts" and the system will adjust its curation logic accordingly — iterating on feed design the way you might refine a prompt with a capable AI assistant. The result is a feed that reflects individual preference with a specificity that platform-level algorithms, optimising for broad engagement, can never match.

Background and Context

Bluesky emerged from a Twitter initiative to build a decentralised social protocol and has since grown into an independent company with a genuine user base, particularly among tech-adjacent and media communities. Its AT Protocol provides a federated identity system — users own their handle and can theoretically migrate between services — alongside an open feed system where anyone can build and publish custom feed algorithms called "Feed Generators."

The Feed Generator system is one of Bluesky's most distinctive features. Unlike Twitter/X, where the algorithm is a black box controlled entirely by the platform, or Instagram, where users toggle between chronological and algorithmic modes with no further control, Bluesky's architecture allows third-party developers to build and publish feed algorithms that any user can subscribe to. Since launch, hundreds of custom feeds have been created — "Science Twitter" replicas, niche interest feeds, geographic feeds, and more.

The challenge has always been accessibility. Building a Feed Generator requires coding knowledge — specifically, the ability to write TypeScript and interact with the Bluesky firehose API. This has limited the ecosystem to technically capable users and developers, leaving the majority of Bluesky's growing user base dependent on the handful of popular feeds that others have built. Attie aims to democratise feed creation by replacing code with conversation, letting anyone with a clear idea of what they want to see build a functioning feed algorithm without writing a single line of code.

Why This Matters

The implications of Attie extend well beyond Bluesky's user base. The project is a proof of concept for a new model of platform personalisation: one where the user defines the algorithm in natural language, an AI translates that intent into executable logic, and the resulting filter runs on open infrastructure that the user can inspect, modify, and export.

This stands in sharp contrast to the dominant model of social media algorithmic curation, where engagement optimisation — not user preference — drives content selection. The engagement-optimised algorithm is economically rational for platforms whose revenue depends on advertising: more time on platform means more ad impressions. But it systematically conflicts with user welfare, prioritising outrage and novelty over depth and relevance. Attie's model aligns the algorithm with stated user intent rather than inferred engagement patterns.

The choice of Anthropic's Claude as the underlying AI is interesting from a trust architecture perspective. Anthropic has been aggressive in positioning Claude as a safety-focused model, and its Constitutional AI approach to training is designed to make the model less likely to generate harmful or manipulative content. For a tool that translates user intent into feed algorithms — where the prompt injection risks are real — using a safety-focused model makes sense. It also continues Anthropic's strategy of embedding Claude in third-party products as infrastructure rather than a consumer-facing brand, similar to the way OpenAI's API powers thousands of applications users interact with daily.

For businesses thinking about productivity and communication tools, Attie signals where the next generation of information management is heading. The ability to manage information flows with AI assistance — filtering signal from noise across professional networks, industry publications, and regulatory updates — is increasingly a competitive advantage. Teams running enterprise productivity software environments should be thinking about how similar AI curation capabilities will integrate with their internal knowledge management and communication stacks.

Industry Impact

Attie puts pressure on the major social platforms to reconsider their algorithmic transparency. TikTok, Instagram, and X have all faced sustained regulatory and user pressure to provide more control over feed algorithms. The EU's Digital Services Act requires that large platforms offer users the ability to opt out of algorithmic ranking and use chronological feeds — Attie goes further, offering users the ability to define their own ranking criteria. This sets a new bar for what "user control over algorithms" can mean in practice.

For developers in the Bluesky ecosystem, Attie creates both an opportunity and a question. The opportunity: Attie democratises feed creation, which should expand the total market for custom feeds and increase the value of the Feed Generator ecosystem. The question: if Attie can generate feed algorithms from natural language, does that reduce or eliminate the market for hand-crafted Feed Generators from specialist developers? The answer is probably "both" — Attie will handle the long tail of personal and semi-personal feeds, while specialist developers continue building high-quality public feeds with capabilities that require careful technical design.

Microsoft's investment in AI productivity tools provides relevant context here. The company's Copilot integration with affordable Microsoft Office licence products represents a similar AI-as-interface model — using natural language to translate user intent into productivity actions. The convergence of AI assistants as the interface layer for complex systems is a broad pattern, not specific to social media.

Expert Perspective

Decentralisation researchers and open protocol advocates have responded to Attie with enthusiasm tempered by questions about longevity. The AT Protocol's open nature means that Attie's feed algorithms, once created, persist on the protocol even if Attie the application disappears — a meaningful difference from feeds created on centralised platforms, which disappear when the third-party app that created them loses access. This resilience is a genuine advantage of the decentralised model.

AI ethics researchers have raised questions about the risks of delegating algorithm design to AI systems. Attie translates user intent faithfully, but intent isn't always fully specified — gaps in the user's description of what they want create spaces where the AI makes choices that may or may not align with actual preferences. The iterative refinement model, where users can correct Attie's interpretation over time, is a reasonable mitigation, but it requires active engagement that many users won't provide.

What This Means for Businesses

For businesses with professional presence on Bluesky — particularly in tech, media, research, and financial services where the platform has meaningful professional penetration — Attie opens interesting possibilities for monitoring and curation. Marketing teams can build custom feeds tracking brand mentions, competitor activity, and industry news without developer resources. Research teams can create feeds filtered to specific domains, publication types, or author networks. The democratisation of feed creation has clear professional applications beyond personal use.

For IT and software strategy teams, Attie is a case study in how AI interfaces can unlock the value of complex open systems for non-technical users. The same pattern — natural language as the interface to powerful but previously inaccessible functionality — is already reshaping how users interact with business software. Organisations evaluating genuine Windows 11 key deployments should be assessing which productivity tools in their stack offer similar AI-driven capability democratisation, and planning for a world where technical barriers to complex customisation continue to fall.

Key Takeaways

  • Bluesky's Attie lets users build custom social media feed algorithms using natural language conversation, powered by Anthropic's Claude.
  • The product democratises Bluesky's existing Feed Generator ecosystem, removing the coding barrier from custom feed creation.
  • Attie's model — user-defined intent translated to algorithmic logic running on open infrastructure — sets a new standard for feed control transparency.
  • The choice of Claude reflects the trust architecture considerations inherent in AI systems that influence information flows.
  • AT Protocol's open nature means Attie-generated feeds persist on the protocol independently of the Attie application.
  • Similar AI-as-interface patterns are reshaping productivity software, social platforms, and enterprise tools across the industry.

Looking Ahead

Attie is in early access as of March 2026, with Bluesky's Exploration team planning to gather user feedback and iterate on the product's conversational interface and feed generation quality. The bigger question is whether Attie will attract the broader Bluesky user base beyond the technically curious early adopters — and whether its model of AI-mediated algorithm design will prove influential enough to pressure larger platforms toward similar transparency. If Attie demonstrates meaningful user satisfaction at scale, it will be a compelling data point in ongoing policy debates about algorithmic accountability.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Attie and how does it work?

Attie is an AI assistant from Bluesky's Exploration team that lets users describe the content they want to see in natural language. The AI — powered by Anthropic's Claude — translates those descriptions into feed algorithms running on Bluesky's AT Protocol, creating custom curated feeds without any coding required.

Is Attie available now?

Attie was unveiled at the Atmosphere conference in late March 2026 and is in early access. Bluesky's Exploration team is gathering user feedback before a broader rollout.

How does Attie differ from normal social media algorithms?

Standard social media algorithms optimise for engagement, which often means prioritising outrage and novelty. Attie translates stated user intent into algorithmic logic, aligning the feed with what users say they want rather than what keeps them scrolling longest.

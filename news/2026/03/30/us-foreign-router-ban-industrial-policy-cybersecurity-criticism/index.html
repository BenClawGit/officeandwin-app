America's Foreign Router Ban Is Industrial Policy Dressed Up as Cybersecurity

By OfficeandWin Tech Desk · 2026-03-30

⚡ Quick Summary

  • A University of Georgia policy professor argues the US foreign router ban is industrial policy dressed up as cybersecurity.
  • The ban targets Chinese-manufactured SOHO routers but lacks the evidence-based, targeted approach that effective security policy requires.
  • Critics argue Netgear's lobbying goals align more closely with the ban's structure than its stated security rationale.
  • Businesses should address remote worker network security through endpoint protection and VPN policies rather than router manufacturer restrictions.

A prominent public policy professor has delivered a sharp rebuke of the United States' ban on foreign-manufactured SOHO routers, arguing that the policy will make American networks less secure — not more — while serving primarily as a vehicle for domestic manufacturer lobbying. The critique cuts to the heart of a troubling pattern in how cybersecurity policy is being made in Washington.

What Happened

Milton Mueller, Professor at the University of Georgia's School of Public Policy and founder of its Internet Governance Project, published a detailed analysis arguing that the FCC's ban on foreign-made small office/home office (SOHO) routers is "industrial policy disguised as cybersecurity." The ban, which targets equipment from Chinese manufacturers including TP-Link and Huawei-affiliated brands, has been framed by its proponents as a necessary security measure to protect critical home and small business network infrastructure from potential Chinese state surveillance and interference.

Mueller's critique is methodical and damning. He argues that the ban conflates two distinct problems — the genuine security risks associated with any network equipment and the specific, evidence-dependent question of whether particular manufacturers have built backdoors or surveillance capabilities into their products — and uses the conflation to justify protectionist policy that benefits domestic manufacturers, particularly Netgear, which has been active in lobbying for foreign router restrictions.

The FCC's action follows a broader pattern of US technology decoupling efforts that have included Huawei's effective exclusion from the US telecommunications market, restrictions on Chinese semiconductor access, and ongoing scrutiny of other Chinese technology companies. The router ban extends this logic to consumer and small business networking equipment — a much broader and more diffuse market than the enterprise telecommunications infrastructure that earlier restrictions targeted.

Background and Context

The security concerns around foreign-manufactured networking equipment are real and not invented by industry lobbyists. Intelligence agencies in the US, UK, Australia, and elsewhere have documented cases where network equipment manufacturers — not exclusively Chinese — have built capabilities into their products that could be exploited by nation-state actors. The Five Eyes intelligence alliance has issued repeated warnings about the risks of using equipment from manufacturers with close ties to authoritarian governments.

However, the leap from "some foreign equipment presents security risks" to "ban all foreign-manufactured SOHO routers" is a substantial one that Mueller and other critics argue hasn't been adequately justified. TP-Link, for instance, is the dominant SOHO router manufacturer globally, with a substantial share of the US home and small business market. The company has publicly pushed back on characterisations of its equipment as security risks and has offered to subject its products and source code to third-party security audits — an offer that, critics note, would be a more targeted and evidence-based approach to addressing legitimate concerns than a blanket ban.

The lobbying dimension adds uncomfortable texture to the policy debate. Netgear, a US-based manufacturer that stands to benefit substantially from the exclusion of its primary competitor, has been a visible presence in Washington arguing for foreign router restrictions. Mueller's analysis suggests that the policy outcome — a broad ban rather than targeted security requirements — maps more closely to Netgear's commercial interests than to the security rationale offered in public justifications.

Why This Matters

The router ban debate matters for cybersecurity policy for several reasons that go beyond the immediate question of which devices consumers can buy. It illustrates a recurring pathology in how security justifications are deployed to advance industrial policy goals — a pattern that becomes more dangerous as the security threat landscape becomes more complex and the stakes of supply chain decisions grow higher.

Effective cybersecurity policy requires evidence-based, targeted interventions. When security rationales are stretched to cover broad protectionist measures, two harms follow. First, the actual security problem — whatever genuinely motivated the initial concern — may not be addressed, because the policy response is calibrated to commercial rather than security outcomes. Second, the credibility of legitimate security interventions is damaged, making it harder to build consensus around measures that are actually necessary.

Mueller's specific argument about the ban making America less secure deserves attention. His reasoning is that SOHO routers are a genuine security concern regardless of manufacturer — they run complex software on devices that rarely receive security updates, they sit at the perimeter of home and small business networks, and they're frequently attacked. The policy response that would most improve security is mandating robust security update policies, vulnerability disclosure requirements, and minimum security standards for all routers sold in the US market — applied regardless of manufacturer origin. Instead, the ban focuses on manufacturer nationality, which is a poor proxy for security quality and leaves the underlying security problems unaddressed.

For businesses managing network infrastructure — including the home office setups that have become central to how knowledge workers connect to enterprise productivity software — the policy creates real practical challenges. If the ban results in reduced competition and higher prices for SOHO equipment without corresponding improvements in security standards, the net effect on business security posture may be negative.

Industry Impact

The short-term commercial impact of the foreign router ban is clear: US prices for SOHO routers will increase as the market loses its dominant supplier and remaining domestic alternatives compete in a less competitive environment. Netgear, ASUS (Taiwanese, and apparently not targeted by the ban), and other non-Chinese manufacturers will benefit. TP-Link, which had captured significant US market share through a combination of price competitiveness and decent product quality, faces a severe commercial blow to its US operations.

The longer-term security implications are murkier. If the ban prompts a genuine improvement in the security practices of US-market routers — through competition among remaining vendors on security features, or through companion regulatory requirements for security update commitments — it could have a net positive effect. If it simply redistributes market share without improving security standards, it achieves Netgear's lobbying goals without achieving its stated security rationale.

For enterprise IT administrators who manage large numbers of remote worker endpoints — including home routers that sit between corporate VPNs and the open internet — the policy debate is largely academic. The practical security response for corporate environments is to enforce VPN usage, deploy endpoint security software on company-managed devices, and conduct regular security awareness training. The specific router model an employee uses at home is a distant concern compared to these more controllable factors. Workers running genuine Windows 11 key deployments with proper endpoint security are protected by layers of defence that make the router's manufacturer largely irrelevant to their security posture.

Expert Perspective

Mueller's critique has found support from an unexpected range of commentators. Free trade advocates predictably oppose the ban on commercial grounds. But security researchers who lack any financial stake in the outcome have also been skeptical of the blanket ban approach. Researchers at academic security labs who've studied SOHO router vulnerabilities note that the worst security offenders in their research have not been exclusively or even predominantly Chinese manufacturers — poor update practices, hardcoded credentials, and unpatched CVEs are industry-wide problems.

The Electronic Frontier Foundation and similar digital rights organisations have argued that security-justified bans on foreign technology products create dangerous precedents. If the security rationale can justify banning foreign routers, the same logic applies to smartphones, software, cloud services, and eventually to the open architecture of the internet itself. Drawing principled lines around when national security justifies technology exclusion is a critical policy challenge that the router ban debate doesn't resolve — it simply advances the front line of the argument.

What This Means for Businesses

For small and medium businesses that have relied on TP-Link and similar Chinese-manufactured routers for their cost-effective performance, the practical path forward is to plan for higher equipment costs and evaluate whether the security features of available alternatives justify the premium. Businesses in regulated industries — finance, healthcare, legal services — should use this moment to audit their network security practices and confirm that their endpoint protection doesn't depend on router security as a primary defence layer.

IT administrators managing distributed teams should ensure remote workers have clear guidance on network security practices: VPN usage policies, the importance of keeping router firmware updated regardless of manufacturer, and secure Wi-Fi configuration requirements. Teams using affordable Microsoft Office licence deployments with cloud storage should confirm that sensitive documents are protected by application-layer encryption and access controls, not solely by network perimeter security.

Key Takeaways

  • A University of Georgia policy professor argues the US foreign router ban is "industrial policy disguised as cybersecurity," primarily benefiting Netgear rather than American security.
  • The ban targets SOHO routers from Chinese manufacturers including TP-Link, framed as a national security measure but criticised for stretching that rationale beyond the evidence.
  • Effective router security policy would mandate security update commitments and minimum standards for all manufacturers — regardless of origin.
  • The ban may increase SOHO router prices in the US without proportionate improvements in actual security outcomes.
  • For enterprise IT, remote worker network security should be addressed through endpoint protection and VPN policies, not router manufacturer restrictions.
  • The policy debate illustrates a broader risk: security justifications being deployed to advance industrial policy goals, damaging the credibility of legitimate security interventions.

Looking Ahead

The foreign router ban is likely to face legal challenges from affected manufacturers and trade partners, and the debate over its security merits will continue in both regulatory and academic settings. More broadly, it's a preview of the policy battles that will intensify as the US-China technology decoupling deepens: where to draw lines, how to justify them, and how to ensure that security rationales drive outcomes rather than commercial interests. Getting these answers right matters for both US security and for the health of the global technology ecosystem that businesses and consumers depend on.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the US foreign router ban?

The FCC has banned the sale of SOHO (small office/home office) routers from certain foreign manufacturers, primarily Chinese companies like TP-Link. The policy is framed as a national security measure to protect home and small business networks from potential Chinese state surveillance.

Why is the router ban controversial?

Critics, including policy researchers, argue the ban is too broad and conflates genuine security concerns with commercial protectionism. They argue effective security policy would mandate security standards for all manufacturers, not ban specific nationalities. The active lobbying by Netgear, which benefits commercially, has raised questions about whether security or industrial policy is the real driver.

How should businesses respond to the router ban?

Focus on endpoint security, VPN usage policies, and ensuring remote workers keep firmware updated regardless of device manufacturer. Don't rely on router perimeter security as a primary defence — application-layer encryption and access controls in productivity software provide more reliable protection.

AI Startups Are Using Dual-Price Equity Deals to Inflate Valuations — Here's Why It Matters

A growing number of artificial intelligence startups are employing a controversial fundraising mechanism that allows them to sell identical equity at two vastly different price points — effectively manufacturing unicorn status while obscuring the true economic picture for later-stage investors and the broader market.

What Happened

According to a recent investigation by TechCrunch, several prominent AI startups have begun structuring their fundraising rounds with a dual-pricing mechanism. In these deals, early investors — often insiders, strategic partners, or existing shareholders — are offered equity at a significantly lower price per share than what headline-grabbing later investors pay. The result is a blended valuation that looks far more impressive in press releases than the underlying economics might justify.

The practice has accelerated throughout late 2025 and into early 2026, coinciding with a renewed surge of venture capital interest in generative AI, large language models, and AI infrastructure plays. Some founders are reportedly using structured notes, warrant kickers, or tiered share classes to create what amounts to a two-track pricing system within a single funding round.

While dual-class share structures are not new in Silicon Valley, the specific application of selling effectively identical economic rights at dramatically different prices within the same round represents a novel twist that has caught the attention of regulators, governance advocates, and competing startups alike.

Background and Context

The venture capital ecosystem has always operated on a certain degree of valuation theatre. Headline valuations are as much marketing tools as they are financial metrics — a $1 billion "unicorn" tag carries enormous signalling power for recruiting, customer acquisition, and subsequent fundraising rounds.

However, the current AI boom has introduced new pressures. With hundreds of AI startups competing for a finite pool of engineering talent and enterprise customers, the perceived valuation of a company has become a critical competitive weapon. Engineers choosing between offers at two different AI labs will often factor in the implied trajectory suggested by a higher valuation. Enterprise buyers evaluating vendors want assurance that their chosen partner will survive and thrive.

The dual-pricing mechanism exploits this dynamic by allowing founders to claim a sky-high valuation — based on the price paid by the most optimistic (or strategically motivated) investor — while quietly offering more favorable terms to others. This creates a gap between the "sticker price" valuation and the actual weighted-average cost of capital raised in the round.

Historically, similar dynamics have played out in real estate, structured finance, and even public equity markets through mechanisms like convertible notes with varying caps. The difference in the current AI landscape is the scale, the speed, and the degree to which these inflated valuations are being used to drive real-world business decisions.

Why This Matters

This trend matters because it fundamentally distorts the information environment around AI company valuations — and that distortion has cascading effects across the entire technology ecosystem. When a startup claims a $5 billion valuation based on a small tranche of high-priced shares while the majority of capital was raised at a $2 billion effective price, every stakeholder making decisions based on that $5 billion number is working with flawed data.

For businesses evaluating enterprise productivity software and AI tools, inflated valuations can create a false sense of stability. A company that appears to be a well-capitalized unicorn may actually be burning through runway faster than its true valuation would support, creating vendor risk for enterprises that have integrated its tools into critical workflows.

The practice also raises serious questions about fiduciary responsibility. Later-stage investors who pay the higher price point may not fully understand that earlier investors in the same round received significantly better terms. While sophisticated institutional investors typically conduct thorough due diligence, the pressure to deploy capital in a hot AI market can lead to shortcuts.

Moreover, this trend risks poisoning the well for legitimate AI companies that are building genuine value. When the market eventually corrects — as it always does — the revelation that many "unicorn" valuations were architecturally inflated could trigger a broader crisis of confidence in AI investments, punishing honest actors alongside the manipulators.

Industry Impact

The ripple effects of dual-pricing equity are already being felt across the technology industry. Competing startups that refuse to engage in valuation engineering find themselves at a perceived disadvantage in recruiting and partnerships, creating pressure to adopt similar practices or risk falling behind in the talent war.

Venture capital firms are increasingly splitting into two camps: those willing to participate in headline-driven pricing (often for strategic reasons or portfolio signalling) and those demanding more transparent deal structures. This bifurcation is reshaping LP-GP relationships, as limited partners push back against managers whose portfolio valuations may be built on questionable foundations.

The enterprise software market is also feeling the effects. Companies evaluating AI vendors — whether for document automation, code generation, or data analytics — must now add "valuation integrity" to their due diligence checklist. A vendor's financial stability cannot be assumed from press releases alone; organizations investing in tools like affordable Microsoft Office licence solutions alongside AI tools need confidence that their AI vendors will still be operational in two years.

Regulatory attention is also mounting. The SEC has historically taken a light touch with private company valuations, but the systemic risk of widespread valuation manipulation in a sector as consequential as AI may force a reconsideration of disclosure requirements for late-stage private companies.

Expert Perspective

Governance experts and veteran venture capitalists have expressed growing concern about the practice. The fundamental issue is one of transparency: when the same equity is priced differently for different investors within a single round, the resulting headline valuation becomes more of a marketing number than a financial one.

Industry observers note that this pattern has historical precedents that ended badly. The late-stage private company valuation inflation of 2015-2016 — when companies like Theranos and WeWork used creative structuring to maintain sky-high valuations — ultimately resulted in spectacular collapses that damaged investor confidence for years. The question is whether the AI sector's genuine technological breakthroughs are sufficient to sustain these valuations even on a corrected basis, or whether the industry is building on a foundation of financial engineering rather than fundamental value.

What This Means for Businesses

For business leaders and IT decision-makers, this trend carries practical implications that extend well beyond the venture capital world. When selecting AI vendors and tools, organizations should look beyond headline valuations and funding announcements to assess actual revenue, customer retention, and unit economics.

The safest strategy for enterprises is to maintain a diversified technology stack that doesn't create single points of failure around any one AI vendor. Pairing AI tools with proven, stable platforms — such as securing a genuine Windows 11 key for core infrastructure — ensures business continuity regardless of what happens in the AI startup ecosystem.

Small and medium businesses should be particularly cautious, as they typically lack the resources to rapidly migrate away from a failed vendor. Building on established platforms while selectively adopting AI enhancements from well-capitalized providers remains the prudent approach.

Key Takeaways

  • Multiple AI startups are selling identical equity at two different price points within single funding rounds to inflate headline valuations
  • The practice creates a gap between reported valuations and actual weighted-average cost of capital
  • Enterprises should assess AI vendor stability based on revenue and unit economics, not funding announcements
  • Regulatory scrutiny of private AI company valuations is likely to increase
  • The trend risks triggering a broader crisis of confidence in AI investments when corrections occur
  • Diversifying technology stacks across proven platforms and selective AI tools is the safest enterprise strategy

Looking Ahead

As the AI boom continues to accelerate through 2026, the tension between genuine innovation and financial engineering will intensify. The startups that build sustainable businesses on real revenue and customer value will ultimately be distinguished from those that relied on creative valuation mechanisms. For the broader technology ecosystem, the lesson is familiar but worth repeating: when something seems too good to be true — whether it's a startup's valuation or its product claims — it usually is. The companies that survive the inevitable correction will be those that prioritized substance over spectacle.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is dual-price equity in AI startups?

Dual-price equity refers to a fundraising practice where AI startups sell effectively identical shares at two different price points within the same funding round, allowing them to claim a higher headline valuation based on the premium-priced tranche.

Why are AI startups inflating their valuations?

Inflated valuations serve as competitive weapons for recruiting top engineering talent, attracting enterprise customers, and securing favorable terms in subsequent funding rounds in the intensely competitive AI market.

How does AI startup valuation inflation affect businesses?

Businesses relying on AI vendors with inflated valuations face vendor risk if those companies cannot sustain their burn rates, making it important to diversify technology stacks and assess vendors on revenue rather than funding announcements.
